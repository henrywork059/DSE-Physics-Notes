1. State variables and absolute temperature

  • Pressure \( p \): force per unit area on the container wall due to gas molecule collisions (unit: Pa).
  • Volume \( V \): volume of the gas (unit: \( \text{m}^3 \)).
  • Temperature \( T \): use Kelvin in gas-law calculations (unit: K).

Kelvin scale (must know)

  • Conversion: $$ T(\text{K}) = t(^\circ\text{C}) + 273 $$
  • Absolute zero is approximately \( -273^\circ\text{C} \) (where \( T = 0\,\text{K} \)).
HKDSE Reminder
Gas laws use absolute temperature. If you keep \( ^\circ\text{C} \), the proportionality graphs will not pass through the origin.

2. Boyle’s law (constant temperature)

  • For a fixed mass of gas at constant temperature: $$ pV = \text{constant} $$
  • So \( p \propto \frac{1}{V} \).

Graphs you should recognise

  • \( p \) vs \( V \): a decreasing curve (hyperbola).
  • \( p \) vs \( 1/V \): a straight line through the origin.
Typical exam step
If \( V \) decreases to half at constant \( T \), then \( p \) doubles.

3. Charles’ law (constant pressure)

  • For a fixed mass of gas at constant pressure: $$ \frac{V}{T} = \text{constant} $$
  • So \( V \propto T \) (with \( T \) in K).

Graph features

  • \( V \) vs \( T(\text{K}) \): straight line through the origin.
  • \( V \) vs \( t(^\circ\text{C}) \): straight line that extrapolates to \( V = 0 \) at about \( -273^\circ\text{C} \).
Meaning of the intercept
The \( -273^\circ\text{C} \) intercept is evidence for the absolute zero concept.

4. Pressure law (constant volume)

  • For a fixed mass of gas at constant volume: $$ \frac{p}{T} = \text{constant} $$
  • So \( p \propto T \) (with \( T \) in K).

Graph features

  • \( p \) vs \( T(\text{K}) \): straight line through the origin.
  • \( p \) vs \( t(^\circ\text{C}) \): straight line that extrapolates to \( p = 0 \) at about \( -273^\circ\text{C} \).
Particle explanation (short)
Higher \( T \) → molecules move faster → more frequent / harder collisions with the wall → larger \( p \).

5. Combined gas law and ideal gas equation

Combined gas law (fixed mass)

  • For a fixed mass of gas: $$ \frac{pV}{T} = \text{constant} $$
  • So: $$ \frac{p_1V_1}{T_1} = \frac{p_2V_2}{T_2} $$
HKDSE Method
Write down \( \frac{p_1V_1}{T_1} = \frac{p_2V_2}{T_2} \) first, then substitute numbers after converting temperature to K.

Ideal gas equation

  • Ideal gas equation: $$ pV = nRT $$
  • \( n \): amount of gas (mol), \( R \): molar gas constant (given when needed).
  • If \( n \) is constant, \( \frac{pV}{T} \) is constant → consistent with the combined gas law.

Quick example (marking-scheme style)

  • A gas changes from \( p_1=1.2\times 10^5\,\text{Pa} \), \( V_1=2.0\times 10^{-3}\,\text{m}^3 \), \( t_1=27^\circ\text{C} \) to \( p_2=1.0\times 10^5\,\text{Pa} \), \( t_2=57^\circ\text{C} \). Find \( V_2 \).
  • Convert: \( T_1 = 300\,\text{K} \), \( T_2 = 330\,\text{K} \).
  • $$ \frac{p_1V_1}{T_1} = \frac{p_2V_2}{T_2} \Rightarrow V_2 = \frac{p_1V_1T_2}{p_2T_1} = \frac{(1.2\times 10^5)(2.0\times 10^{-3})(330)}{(1.0\times 10^5)(300)} = 2.64\times 10^{-3}\,\text{m}^3 $$

6. Key terms

Pressure \( p \): force per unit area; in gases it is due to molecule collisions with the container wall (unit: Pa).

Volume \( V \): space occupied by the gas (unit: \( \text{m}^3 \)).

Absolute temperature \( T \): temperature in kelvin. \( T(\text{K}) = t(^\circ\text{C}) + 273 \).

Boyle’s law: for a fixed mass of gas at constant temperature, \( pV = \text{constant} \) (so \( p \propto 1/V \)).

Charles’ law: for a fixed mass of gas at constant pressure, \( V/T = \text{constant} \) (so \( V \propto T \)).

Pressure law: for a fixed mass of gas at constant volume, \( p/T = \text{constant} \) (so \( p \propto T \)).

Combined gas law: for a fixed mass of gas, \( \frac{pV}{T} = \text{constant} \), i.e. \( \frac{p_1V_1}{T_1} = \frac{p_2V_2}{T_2} \).

Ideal gas equation: \( pV = nRT \), where \( n \) is amount in mol and \( R \) is the molar gas constant.

7. Common traps (high-frequency DSE mistakes)

Warning / Trap
  • Using \( ^\circ\text{C} \) in ratios:
    Fix: always convert to kelvin: \( T = t + 273 \).
  • Mixing up the constant condition:
    Fix: Boyle (constant \( T \)), Charles (constant \( p \)), Pressure law (constant \( V \)).
  • Wrong graph conclusion:
    Fix: \( p \) vs \( V \) is not a straight line; use \( p \) vs \( 1/V \) for a straight line.
  • Forgetting units:
    Fix: use Pa for pressure and \( \text{m}^3 \) for volume unless the question keeps consistent units.
  • Thinking \( pV/T \) changes for the same mass:
    Fix: for a fixed mass (fixed \( n \)), \( pV/T \) is constant (ideal-gas behaviour).
  • Sign error for temperature change:
    Fix: “increase by \( 10^\circ\text{C} \)” means \( T \) increases by 10 K (same increment), but ratios must still use kelvin.
