Compulsory — Internal Energy, Heat & Temperature

HKDSE Physics — Compulsory Part · Distinguish internal energy, heat and temperature, and explain energy transfer in heating/cooling processes.

1. Temperature, heat and their difference

  • Temperature indicates the degree of hotness of a body (related to particle motion).
  • Heat is energy transferred due to a temperature difference (from higher \(T\) to lower \(T\)).
  • Heat is not stored in a body. A body stores internal energy, not “heat”.
  • Unit: heat is energy, so unit is joule (J).
HKDSE Reminder
In explanations, use: “heat is transferred from … to …” and “internal energy increases/decreases”. Avoid “heat is contained”.

2. Internal energy \(U\) (particle model)

  • Internal energy \(U\) is the total microscopic energy of the particles in a body.
  • It consists of:
    • Kinetic energy of random motion of particles.
    • Potential energy due to intermolecular forces (separation between particles).
  • Raising temperature generally increases the average kinetic energy of particles, so \(U\) increases.
  • During a change of state, temperature may stay constant while \(U\) changes mainly because potential energy changes.
Exam Link
Typical marking point: “When heated, particles move faster → average kinetic energy increases → internal energy increases.”

3. Heating and cooling (what changes?)

Heating with temperature rise

  • Heat transferred into the body increases internal energy.
  • Particles gain kinetic energy → temperature rises.

Heating during change of state

  • Temperature can remain constant while heat is absorbed.
  • Energy is used to overcome intermolecular forces → potential energy increases → internal energy increases.
Language Tip
Use “heat absorbed/released” for energy transfer, and “internal energy changes” for energy stored in the substance.
Common Misconception
“If temperature is constant, no heat is transferred” is wrong. During melting/boiling, heat is absorbed while temperature stays constant.

4. Thermal equilibrium and direction of heat transfer

  • When two bodies at different temperatures are in contact, heat is transferred from the higher-temperature body to the lower-temperature body.
  • Thermal equilibrium is reached when there is no net heat transfer between them.
  • At thermal equilibrium, the two bodies have the same temperature.
HKDSE Reminder
“No net heat transfer” does not mean “no microscopic exchange”; it means overall energy transfer balances, so temperatures stay constant.

5. Key terms

Internal energy \(U\): total microscopic kinetic energy + potential energy of the particles in a body.

Heat \(Q\): energy transferred due to a temperature difference (from higher \(T\) to lower \(T\)); unit is joule (J).

Temperature \(T\): a measure of the degree of hotness; related to the random motion of particles.

Change in internal energy: internal energy increases when the body absorbs heat; it decreases when the body releases heat.

Change of state: a process (e.g. melting/boiling) where temperature may remain constant while internal energy changes mainly due to change in potential energy.

Heat transfer mechanisms: conduction, convection and radiation are ways thermal energy can be transferred (details learned in separate notes).

Thermal equilibrium: a state where two bodies in contact have the same temperature so there is no net heat transfer.

6. Common traps

Warning / Trap
  • Heat vs temperature: temperature is a state; heat is energy in transfer due to temperature difference.
  • “Heat stored in an object”: wrong. The object stores internal energy.
  • Constant temperature means no heat transfer: wrong during change of state.
  • Internal energy depends only on temperature: not always; it also depends on particle separation (potential energy), especially in change of state.
  • Direction mistake: heat flows from higher \(T\) to lower \(T\) until thermal equilibrium.
