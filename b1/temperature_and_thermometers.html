1. Temperature and what a thermometer measures

Core ideas

  • Temperature: a measure of the degree of hotness of an object.
  • A thermometer measures temperature using a property that changes with temperature.
  • For a correct reading, the thermometer must reach thermal equilibrium with the object.
Reminder
Do not confuse temperature with heat energy. Heat is energy transferred due to a temperature difference; temperature is a state of hotness.

Practical reading rule

  • Wait until the reading becomes steady (no longer changing) before recording the temperature.
  • Ensure good thermal contact between the thermometer and the object (e.g. immerse the bulb fully in liquid, without touching the container).

2. Thermometric (temperature-dependent) property

What makes a good thermometric property?

  • Changes noticeably with temperature (good sensitivity).
  • Gives one unique value for each temperature (no ambiguity).
  • Preferably varies linearly with temperature within the working range.

Common examples (HKDSE)

  • Liquid-in-glass thermometer: volume of liquid (thermal expansion).
  • Resistance thermometer / thermistor thermometer: electrical resistance.
  • Thermocouple: e.m.f. produced at a junction.
  • Infra-red thermometer: intensity of infra-red radiation emitted by the object.
Warning / Trap
A property is not suitable if the same reading can occur at two different temperatures (ambiguous), even if it changes with temperature.

Improving sensitivity (typical 1–2 marks)

  • Liquid-in-glass: narrower capillary tube and/or larger bulb → larger length change per \(1^\circ\text{C}\).
  • But higher sensitivity may reduce the measurable range and increase fragility.

3. Celsius temperature scale and fixed points

Two fixed points

  • Ice point (lower fixed point): temperature of pure melting ice at normal atmospheric pressure.
  • Steam point (upper fixed point): temperature of steam over pure boiling water at normal atmospheric pressure.

How the scale is defined

  • Assign ice point as \(0^\circ\text{C}\) and steam point as \(100^\circ\text{C}\).
  • Divide the interval between them into 100 equal divisions; each division is \(1^\circ\text{C}\).
  • Temperatures can be below \(0^\circ\text{C}\) or above \(100^\circ\text{C}\).
HKDSE Note
Fixed points depend on pressure. Always state “at normal atmospheric pressure” when defining the ice point and steam point.

Kelvin (absolute) temperature

$$ T(\text{K}) = \theta(^\circ\text{C}) + 273 $$
  • Kelvin is used in many thermal physics equations (absolute scale).
  • \(0\,\text{K}\) corresponds to absolute zero (about \(-273^\circ\text{C}\)).

4. Calibrating a thermometer and using calibration graphs

Calibrating an unmarked liquid-in-glass thermometer

  • Place the thermometer in pure melting ice; mark the liquid level (ice point).
  • Place it in steam over pure boiling water; mark the liquid level (steam point).
  • Divide the distance between the two marks into 100 equal divisions → \(1^\circ\text{C}\) per division.

Calibration graph & proportional method

If the thermometric property varies linearly with temperature, you can use a straight-line calibration graph or proportion.

$$ \frac{x_T - x_0}{x_{100} - x_0} = \frac{T - 0}{100 - 0} = \frac{T}{100} $$
  • \(x\) can be length of liquid column \(L\), resistance \(R\), e.m.f., etc.
  • From the graph: read the corresponding temperature for a measured \(x_T\).
  • From proportion: rearrange to find \(T\) directly.
Common mistake
Using the linear formula beyond the calibrated range or when the \(x\)–\(T\) relation is not linear will give a wrong temperature.

Exam-style add-on: “thermometer calibrated incorrectly”

  • If a thermometer gives wrong readings at the fixed points, treat those readings as \(x_0\) and \(x_{100}\) and use linear interpolation to find the true temperature.
  • Always state the key assumption: the thermometric property varies linearly with temperature.

5. Types of thermometers and how to choose

Match thermometer to purpose

  • Consider range (low/high temperatures), sensitivity, response time, and practicality.
  • Different thermometers use different thermometric properties.
Thermometer Key point (HKDSE wording)
Liquid-in-glass Uses thermal expansion (volume of liquid). Simple and direct, but limited range and slower response.
Resistance thermometer Uses resistance of a metal wire (e.g. platinum). Can be sensitive and suitable for precise measurement if \(R\) varies steadily with \(T\).
Thermistor thermometer Uses resistance of a thermistor. Often very sensitive over a limited range; may be non-linear.
Thermocouple Uses e.m.f. at junctions of two metals. Suitable for a wide range including high temperatures; fast response.
Infra-red thermometer Uses intensity of infra-red radiation emitted; no contact needed (useful for screening and moving objects).
Selection tip
For high temperatures, a liquid-in-glass thermometer may fail; choose a thermometer designed for high-temperature measurement (e.g. thermocouple).

6. Key terms

Temperature: a measure of the degree of hotness of an object.

Thermometer: an instrument that measures temperature by using a temperature-dependent property.

Thermometric (temperature-dependent) property: a physical property that changes with temperature and can be used to indicate temperature (e.g. length of liquid column, resistance, e.m.f.).

Fixed points (Celsius scale): two reference temperatures used to define the scale; the interval is divided into 100 equal divisions.

Ice point: temperature of pure melting ice at normal atmospheric pressure (lower fixed point, \(0^\circ\text{C}\)).

Steam point: temperature of steam over pure boiling water at normal atmospheric pressure (upper fixed point, \(100^\circ\text{C}\)).

Calibration: marking a thermometer scale (or drawing a calibration graph) using known reference temperatures, so unknown temperatures can be determined from the measured thermometric property.

Sensitivity: the change in thermometer reading per unit temperature change (e.g. length change per \(1^\circ\text{C}\)); higher sensitivity helps detect small temperature changes.

7. Common traps (high-frequency DSE mistakes)

Warning / Trap
  • Wrong fixed points: using ice from a freezer (not pure melting ice) or using boiling water itself (instead of steam over pure boiling water).
  • Missing conditions: not stating “at normal atmospheric pressure” for ice point and steam point.
  • Not waiting for equilibrium: reading before the thermometer becomes steady.
  • Linear assumption abuse: applying \( \frac{x_T-x_0}{x_{100}-x_0}=\frac{T}{100} \) when the \(x\)–\(T\) relation is non-linear or outside the calibrated range.
  • Mixing scales: using Celsius values directly in Kelvin-only equations; remember \(T(\text{K})=\theta(^\circ\text{C})+273\).
  • Poor calibration practice: impurities in ice/water or touching the beaker wall causes a wrong fixed-point reading.
