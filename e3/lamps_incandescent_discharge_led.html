E3 — Lamps: Incandescent, Gas Discharge & LED

HKDSE Physics — Elective E3: Energy & Use of Energy · Compare lamps using luminous efficacy, understand how each lamp produces light, and explain energy-saving choices.

← Back to index

In E3, you should be able to compare different lamps by their luminous efficacy (lm W\(^{-1}\)) and explain why some lamps waste more energy as heat (infra-red).

Exam focus: luminous efficacy, energy conversion (light vs heat), basic working principles Skills: compare by same brightness (luminous flux) or same power; relate low efficacy to heat loss

1. Syllabus focus (what you must be able to do)

  • Compare different lamps in terms of energy efficiency (using luminous efficacy).
  • Describe the main energy conversions in different lamps (light vs heat loss).
  • State basic working principles of incandescent, gas discharge and LED lamps (qualitative).
Exam habit
Always link your choice to higher luminous efficacy (more light output per watt) and therefore lower electricity consumption for the same brightness.

2. Key comparison idea (E3 core)

Energy conversion in lamps

  • All lamps convert electrical energy into electromagnetic energy (light) and internal energy (heating).
  • Energy wasted mainly appears as infra-red radiation (heat) and heating of the lamp / surroundings.

How to compare fairly

  • Compare lamps with the same light output (same luminous flux) — the one with smaller power uses less electricity.
  • Or compare lamps with the same power — the one with larger light output is more energy efficient.
Common trap
Do not compare brightness by “watt”. Watt is power input, not light output. Use luminous flux (lm) or illuminance (lux) if provided.

3. Incandescent (filament) lamp

How it produces light

  • A thin filament has a high resistance.
  • When current passes through it, the filament becomes very hot.
  • It emits electromagnetic radiation: visible light + a lot of infra-red.

Energy efficiency (qualitative)

  • Large proportion of energy is emitted as infra-red (heat), so luminous efficacy is low.
  • The lamp becomes hot during operation.
Exam wording
“Low luminous efficacy because most electrical energy is converted into heat (infra-red) rather than visible light.”

4. Gas discharge lamp (e.g. fluorescent)

Main idea

  • An electric discharge through a gas can excite atoms / molecules in the gas.
  • When they de-excite, they emit electromagnetic radiation.

Fluorescent lamp (typical example)

  • Discharge in mercury vapour produces mainly ultraviolet (UV) radiation.
  • A phosphor coating on the tube absorbs UV and emits visible light (fluorescence).
Why more efficient than filament
More of the electrical energy can end up as visible light (after conversion from UV), so luminous efficacy is usually higher than an incandescent lamp.
Do not over-detail
HKDSE typically needs only a qualitative description (discharge excites gas, light emitted / phosphor converts UV to visible). No advanced electronics is required.

5. LED lamp (light-emitting diode)

How it produces light (qualitative)

  • An LED is a semiconductor device that emits light when current passes through it.
  • It can produce visible light efficiently with less energy wasted as heat compared with filament lamps.

Energy efficiency

  • LED lamps generally have high luminous efficacy.
  • They usually stay much cooler than filament lamps for similar brightness.
Energy-saving choice
For the same brightness, an LED typically needs smaller power \(\rightarrow\) less electrical energy used \(\rightarrow\) lower running cost.

6. Summary table (HKDSE-style comparison)

Lamp type How light is produced (key idea) Energy efficiency (typical)
Incandescent (filament) Filament heated by current \(\rightarrow\) emits visible + much infra-red Low luminous efficacy (large heat loss)
Gas discharge (fluorescent) Discharge excites gas \(\rightarrow\) UV; phosphor converts UV \(\rightarrow\) visible light Medium to high (usually higher than filament)
LED Semiconductor device emits light when current flows High luminous efficacy (less heat loss)

7. Reminders & common exam traps

  • Brightness is about light output, not power input. Use lm / lux if given.
  • When asked “which lamp saves energy?”, state: higher luminous efficacy \(\rightarrow\) smaller power for same light output \(\rightarrow\) less electrical energy used.
  • For filament lamps, mention that much energy becomes infra-red (heat).

8. Key terms

  • Incandescent lamp: produces light by heating a filament to a high temperature.
  • Gas discharge lamp: produces light when an electric discharge excites a gas, which then emits radiation.
  • Fluorescence: emission of visible light from a substance after absorbing higher-energy radiation (e.g. UV).
  • LED: light-emitting diode; a semiconductor device that emits light when current passes through it.
  • Infra-red radiation: electromagnetic radiation felt as heat; a major energy loss in filament lamps.
  • Luminous efficacy: light output per unit power input, in \(\text{lm W}^{-1}\).
