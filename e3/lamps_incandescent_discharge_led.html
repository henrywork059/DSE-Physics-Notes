Overview

Lighting technology has evolved from filament bulbs to fluorescent tubes and now to solid‑state light emitting diodes (LEDs). Each lamp type produces light in a different way and has a different energy efficiency and lifespan. The HKDSE syllabus requires students to gather information on incandescent, gas discharge and LED lamps【912087049771804†L5088-L5095】.

It is also important to recognise that the eye’s response depends on wavelength【912087049771804†L5088-L5095】. Lamps that produce more light in the visible spectrum appear brighter for the same radiant power.

Incandescent lamps

An incandescent lamp consists of a tungsten filament inside a glass bulb. When current passes through the filament it heats up to around 2 700 °C and emits thermal radiation; only about 5 % of the electrical energy is converted to visible light, the rest is lost as heat. Typical lifespans are 750–1 000 hours. Because of their low efficacy (≈ 15 lm W−1) these lamps are being phased out in favour of more efficient technologies.

Trap: The brightness of an incandescent bulb depends on both power and efficacy. A higher‑wattage filament bulb is brighter but also wastes more energy as heat.

Gas discharge lamps

Gas discharge lamps, such as fluorescent tubes and compact fluorescent lamps (CFLs), produce light by passing a current through a gas or vapour. The excited atoms emit ultraviolet radiation, which is then converted to visible light by a phosphor coating. These lamps are much more efficient than incandescent bulbs (≈ 60–80 lm W−1) and last 6 000–10 000 hours. Their colour rendering and warm‑up time depend on the gas mixture and phosphor.

Light emitting diodes (LEDs)

LEDs are solid‑state devices. When a forward bias is applied to a semiconductor p‑n junction, electrons and holes recombine and release energy as photons. The wavelength (colour) depends on the band gap of the semiconductor. White LEDs use blue‑emitting chips with a phosphor layer to convert part of the light to longer wavelengths. LEDs have very high efficacies (90–150 lm W−1), instant start‑up and lifespans over 25 000 hours.

Note: LEDs are directional sources. A well‑designed luminaire can take advantage of this to deliver light where it is needed without reflectors.

Reminders & Key terms

Reminders:
  • The eye’s sensitivity peaks at green wavelengths (~550 nm). A lamp’s spectral output affects perceived brightness【912087049771804†L5088-L5095】.
  • Lamp efficacy is measured in lumens per watt (lm W−1). A higher efficacy means more light for the same power.
  • Always recycle CFLs and LEDs according to local guidelines.
Key terms
  • Incandescent lamp – produces light by heating a filament.
  • Gas discharge lamp – uses an electrical discharge through a gas to generate light (e.g. fluorescent).
  • LED – semiconductor device that emits light when current flows.
  • Efficacy – luminous flux per unit electrical power.
