Emission of radiation

All objects at a temperature above absolute zero emit electromagnetic radiation. This energy is carried by photons and does not require a medium to travel — hence radiation can occur through vacuum. The amount of energy radiated per unit area is described by the Stefan–Boltzmann law:

\[P = \sigma \varepsilon A T^4\,,\] where \(\sigma\) is the Stefan–Boltzmann constant, \(\varepsilon\) is the emissivity (between 0 and 1) and \(T\) is the absolute temperature in kelvin. Surfaces with higher emissivity radiate more energy.

Note: The radiation spectrum from a warm object peaks at infrared wavelengths. This is why thermal cameras detect IR.

Absorption and surface properties

When radiation strikes a surface it may be absorbed, reflected or transmitted. The efficiency of absorption depends on the colour and texture of the surface. Dull black surfaces are excellent absorbers and emitters; shiny, light‑coloured surfaces are poor absorbers and reflect much of the incoming radiation.

In building physics this means that dark, matte roofs and walls absorb more solar energy than light‑coloured, reflective finishes. Choosing appropriate materials can therefore reduce unwanted heat gain.

Common trap: Students often confuse thermal radiation with conduction or convection. Remember that radiation does not require a medium and travels at the speed of light.

Applications

Understanding heat radiation allows engineers to design more efficient buildings and appliances. Examples include:

  • Solar control coatings: Window films that reflect infrared radiation while transmitting visible light help reduce cooling loads.
  • Radiator fins: Painting fins matte black increases emissivity, improving heat emission.
  • Thermal insulation: Aluminium foil behind radiators reflects radiation back into the room, reducing heat loss through walls.

Although the HKDSE syllabus does not explicitly test Stefan–Boltzmann calculations, a qualitative understanding of how surface colour and finish affect radiation is useful when discussing building envelopes and energy efficiency.

Reminders

Remember: Heat radiation travels in straight lines and can be reflected. In the context of buildings, shading devices and low‑E coatings help manage radiation without compromising daylight.
