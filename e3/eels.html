E3 — Energy Efficiency Labelling Scheme (EELS)

HKDSE Physics — Elective E3: Energy & Use of Energy · Read energy labels, compare grades (1–5), and solve running cost / power questions.

In EELS questions, you mainly do two things: (1) interpret the label (grade 1–5, energy consumption, assumptions), and (2) calculate running cost / input power using \(E = Pt\).

1. Syllabus focus (what you must be able to do)

  • Discuss the uses of the Hong Kong Energy Efficiency Labelling Scheme (EELS) for energy-saving.
  • Interpret information shown on an energy label (e.g. grade, energy consumption, assumptions).
  • Solve problems involving EELS (e.g. electricity cost, input power, comparing grades).
  • Suggest examples of energy-saving devices.
Note
EELS classifies energy performance into five grades (1 to 5). Grade 1 is the most efficient and Grade 5 is the least efficient in that category.

2. What is EELS?

  • Electrical appliances with high energy efficiencies save energy.
  • Appliances included in EELS are affixed with energy labels to show their energy performance, helping consumers choose more energy-efficient products.
Why it helps
A more efficient appliance usually uses less electrical energy (kW h) for the same purpose, so it gives a lower running cost.

3. Types of energy labels (overview)

  • There are two labelling schemes: voluntary and mandatory.
  • The voluntary scheme includes Grading Type and Recognition Type energy labels.
  • The mandatory scheme includes only Grading Type energy labels.
Recognition type
A Recognition Type label indicates that the product has met the minimum energy efficiency specified in the scheme.

4. How to read an energy label (problem-solving steps)

Step 1: Identify what is given

  • Grade (1–5), or an index/value related to energy performance.
  • Energy consumption over a stated period (often in kW h, e.g. per year under a stated usage assumption).
  • Any usage assumption (e.g. operating time per year).

Step 2: Decide what you need to find

  • Running cost: \(\text{Cost} = (\text{electricity tariff per kW h})\times E(\text{kW h})\).
  • Input power (if energy and time are given): \(P = \dfrac{E}{t}\).
Common trap
Don’t forget the assumption written on the label. For example, if the label states “1460 hours per year”, then the listed kW h is under that assumption.

5. Worked example (television label → cost & input power)

Example: energy label information (television, partial) Given on label
Assumed operating time
1460 h per year
Annual energy consumption
38 kW h
Energy Efficiency Index
0.19
Diagonal screen size
80 cm

(a) Estimate the electricity cost for 1460 h

  • Electricity tariff: \($1.1\) per kW h (given)
  • Energy used: \(E = 38\,\text{kW h}\) (from label)
  • \(\text{Cost} = 1.1 \times 38 = 41.8\)
  • Answer: \(\boxed{\$41.8}\)

(b) Estimate the input power of the television

  • \(P = \dfrac{E}{t} = \dfrac{38\,\text{kW h}}{1460\,\text{h}} = 0.0260\,\text{kW}\)
  • \(0.0260\,\text{kW} = 26\,\text{W}\)
  • Answer: \(\boxed{26\,\text{W}}\)
Tip
If \(E\) is in kW h and \(t\) is in h, then \(P\) comes out in kW directly (no extra unit conversion needed).

6. Energy Efficiency Index (EEI) & grading

Energy Efficiency Index (example definition)

  • Appliances can be graded by an Energy Efficiency Index \(I_{\varepsilon}\):
  • \(\displaystyle I_{\varepsilon} = \frac{E_{ac}}{E_{av}}\times 100\%\)
  • \(E_{ac}\): actual energy consumption of the product
  • \(E_{av}\): average energy consumption of products in the same category
Meaning
Smaller \(I_{\varepsilon}\) means smaller \(E_{ac}\), so the product uses less energy than average in that category.

Example grading table (electric storage water heater)

\(I_{\varepsilon}\) (%) Grade
\(I_{\varepsilon} \le 75\)1
\(75 < I_{\varepsilon} \le 90\)2
\(90 < I_{\varepsilon} \le 105\)3
\(105 < I_{\varepsilon} \le 120\)4
\(120 < I_{\varepsilon}\)5

Worked multiple-choice style idea

  • Compare a grade 2 product with a grade 4 product.
  • For the minimum percentage difference (“at least”), take grade 2 at the highest value (90) and grade 4 at the lowest value (105).
  • \(\text{percent lower} = \dfrac{105 - 90}{105}\times 100\% = 14.3\%\)
  • Answer: \(\boxed{14.3\%}\)
Exam note
The grading boundaries depend on the appliance category. In questions, the necessary table / criteria will be provided.

7. Examples of energy-saving devices (E3 required)

  • Higher-grade appliances in EELS (e.g. grade 1 instead of grade 4–5) for the same category.
  • LED lamps (high lighting efficacy) instead of incandescent lamps.
  • Devices that reduce unnecessary operation time (e.g. timer / automatic switch-off).
How to write in exam
Mention the device and the reason: “It uses less electrical energy (kW h) for the same purpose, so it reduces running cost.”

8. Reminders & common exam traps

  • Compare within the same category: the grade meaning is “most/least efficient in that category”.
  • When asked for running cost, always use energy in kW h.
  • When asked for input power, use \(P=\dfrac{E}{t}\) and keep units consistent (kW h with h → kW).
  • Use the assumption shown on the label (e.g. operating hours per year).
Unit trap
Don’t multiply tariff (per kW h) by energy in joule. Convert to kW h first (or work in kW h directly from the label).

9. Key terms

EELS: Energy Efficiency Labelling Scheme (Hong Kong) — energy labels show energy performance of appliances.

Grading Type label: classifies energy performance into grades 1 to 5.

Recognition Type label: indicates the product meets the minimum energy efficiency specified.

Electricity consumption: energy used by an appliance, often in kW h.

Electricity tariff: price per kW h used for cost calculation.

Energy Efficiency Index \(I_{\varepsilon}\): \(\dfrac{E_{ac}}{E_{av}}\times 100\%\) (in some categories/questions).
