1. Syllabus focus (what you must be able to do)

  • State that electricity is the main source of domestic energy.
  • Describe the energy conversion involved in electrical appliances.
  • Define end-use energy efficiency as the ratio of useful energy output to energy input.
  • Identify \(E_{\text{in}}\) and \(E_{\text{out}}\) correctly for different appliances.
Note
In HKDSE questions, you are usually given data like power rating, time, and heating information. Use the standard formulas \(E = Pt\) and \(E = mc\Delta T\) where appropriate.

2. Electricity as the main source of domestic energy

  • Most household appliances use electricity, so electricity is the main source of domestic energy.
  • When an appliance operates, electrical energy is converted into other forms of energy (useful and not useful).

3. Energy conversions in common appliances

Appliance Energy input Useful energy output Other energy (not useful)
Filament lamp Electrical energy Electromagnetic energy (visible light) Electromagnetic energy (infra-red), internal energy of lamp
Electric kettle Electrical energy Internal energy gained by water Internal energy gained by kettle and surroundings
Electric space heater Electrical energy Internal energy (heating the room) Very small (often taken as nearly all useful)
Common trap
Useful energy output depends on the purpose of the appliance. For a lamp, heat is usually not useful; for a heater, heating is the useful output.

4. End-use energy efficiency \(\eta_{\text{end}}\)

Definition

  • End-use energy efficiency tells the proportion of energy input converted into useful energy output.

Energy Flow Diagram

HKDSE formulas

  • \(\displaystyle \text{End-use energy efficiency} = \frac{\text{useful energy output}}{\text{energy input}} \times 100\%\)
  • \(\displaystyle \eta_{\text{end}} = \frac{E_{\text{out}}}{E_{\text{in}}} \times 100\%\)
  • Since \(P = \dfrac{E}{t}\), you may also use: \(\displaystyle \eta_{\text{end}} = \frac{P_{\text{out}}}{P_{\text{in}}} \times 100\%\)
Caution
\(E_{\text{in}}\) is the energy input to the appliance. Do not include losses in electricity generation or transmission.

5. Worked example (HKDSE style): electric kettle

  • A \(2200\,\text{W}\) electric kettle heats \(1.5\,\text{kg}\) of water from \(25^\circ\text{C}\) to \(100^\circ\text{C}\) in \(4\,\text{min}\).
  • Given: \(c_{\text{water}} = 4200\,\text{J kg}^{-1}\,^\circ\text{C}^{-1}\).

(a) Energy input \(E_{\text{in}}\)

  • \(E_{\text{in}} = Pt = 2200 \times (4\times 60) = 5.28\times 10^5\,\text{J}\)

(b) Useful energy output \(E_{\text{out}}\)

  • Useful output = internal energy gained by water
  • \(E_{\text{out}} = mc\Delta T = 1.5 \times 4200 \times (100-25) = 4.725\times 10^5\,\text{J}\)

(c) End-use energy efficiency

  • \(\displaystyle \eta_{\text{end}} = \frac{E_{\text{out}}}{E_{\text{in}}}\times 100\% = \frac{4.725\times 10^5}{5.28\times 10^5}\times 100\% \approx 89.5\%\)
Reminder
For a kettle, the useful energy is the energy absorbed by the water. Energy gained by the kettle body and surroundings is not useful.

6. Electricity consumption & cost (kW h)

Energy used by an appliance

  • \(E = Pt\)
  • If \(P\) is in kW and \(t\) is in hours, then \(E\) is in \(\text{kW h}\).

Unit conversion

  • \(1\,\text{kW h} = 1000\,\text{W} \times 3600\,\text{s} = 3.6\times 10^6\,\text{J}\)
  • So \(\displaystyle E(\text{kW h}) = \frac{E(\text{J})}{3.6\times 10^6}\).

Cost of electricity

  • \(\text{Cost} = (\text{electricity price per kW h}) \times E(\text{kW h})\)
Worked mini-example
From the kettle example, \(E_{\text{in}} = 5.28\times10^5\,\text{J}\). \(\displaystyle E = \frac{5.28\times10^5}{3.6\times10^6} \approx 0.147\,\text{kW h}\). If electricity costs \($1.1\) per kW h (given), then cost \(\approx 1.1\times 0.147 \approx \$0.16\).
Common trap
Don’t mix units. If you use \(E\) in joule, you must convert it to \(\text{kW h}\) before multiplying by the price per kW h.

7. Reminders & common exam traps

  • State the purpose first (what is useful output?): e.g. lamp → visible light; heater → heating (internal energy).
  • Use \(E = Pt\) for energy input, with \(t\) in seconds if \(P\) is in watt.
  • For heating questions, useful energy is often \(mc\Delta T\) (and you must use the correct \(c\)).
  • End-use energy efficiency is about the appliance only: do not include power station / transmission losses.
  • When calculating cost, work in \(\text{kW h}\) (or convert from joule using \(1\,\text{kW h} = 3.6\times10^6\,\text{J}\)).

8. Key terms

Energy conversion: change of energy from one form to another during appliance operation (e.g., electrical → light + heat).

Useful energy output \(E_{\text{out}}\): the energy in the form we want from the appliance (e.g. light from a lamp).

Energy input \(E_{\text{in}}\): electrical energy supplied to the appliance.

End-use energy efficiency \(\eta_{\text{end}}\): \(\dfrac{E_{\text{out}}}{E_{\text{in}}}\times 100\%\).

Power rating \(P\): rate of energy transfer, \(P = \dfrac{E}{t}\) (unit: W).

kilowatt-hour (kW h): a unit of energy used for electricity billing; \(1\,\text{kW h} = 3.6\times10^6\,\text{J}\).
