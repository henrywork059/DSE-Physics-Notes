Overview

Household electrical appliances convert electrical energy into useful forms such as heat, light or mechanical work. The rate at which an appliance converts energy is called its power rating and is measured in watts (W) or kilowatts (kW). When a device of power \(P\) operates for a time \(t\) it consumes an energy \(E = P \times t\), commonly quoted in kilowatt‑hours (kWh). Electricity bills are based on the total number of kWh used.

The HKDSE curriculum requires students to be able to determine running cost using the power rating and to solve problems involving end‑use energy efficiency of appliances【912087049771804†L5119-L5124】. This page summarises key concepts and provides worked examples.

Energy efficiency

The energy efficiency of an appliance is the ratio of useful output energy to the input electrical energy, usually expressed as a percentage: \[\text{efficiency} = \frac{\text{useful output energy}}{\text{input energy}} \times 100\%\,.\] For example, if an electric kettle converts 0.90 kWh of useful heat for every 1.0 kWh supplied, its efficiency is 90 %.

High‑efficiency appliances waste less energy as heat or sound. The HK curriculum emphasises comparing end‑use energy efficiency between different cooking methods and other home devices【912087049771804†L5119-L5124】.

Note: Efficiency is a ratio, not a measure of output. An appliance with a higher power rating may deliver more useful energy even if its efficiency is lower.
Common trap: Students sometimes confuse efficiency with power. Power refers to the rate of energy conversion; efficiency describes how much of the input becomes useful output.

Running cost

To calculate the running cost of an appliance you need:

  • The power rating \(P\) (in kW).
  • The time of operation \(t\) (in hours).
  • The unit cost of electricity (cents or dollars per kWh).

The energy consumed is \(E = P t\). Multiplying \(E\) by the unit cost gives the total cost. For example, a 0.75 kW rice cooker used for 2 hours consumes \(0.75 \times 2 = 1.5\) kWh. At HK$1.3 per kWh the cost is HK$1.95.

Reminder: 1 kWh is the energy used by a 1 kW device operating for 1 hour. When converting from watts to kilowatts divide by 1000.

Worked example

An air‑conditioner has a power rating of 1.2 kW and an efficiency of 85 %. It runs for 5 hours per day. The electricity tariff is HK$1.3 per kWh. Find (a) the daily energy consumption, (b) the useful cooling energy delivered, and (c) the daily running cost.

Solution:

  1. Energy consumed: \(E = P t = 1.2 \times 5 = 6\) kWh.
  2. Useful cooling energy: \(E_{\text{useful}} = \text{efficiency} \times E = 0.85 \times 6 = 5.1\) kWh.
  3. Cost: \(\text{Cost} = E \times \text{tariff} = 6 \times 1.3 = \text{HK}\$7.8\).

Reminders & Key points

Reminders:
  • When comparing appliances, look at both the power rating and the efficiency.
  • Always convert power to kilowatts and time to hours before calculating kWh.
  • The unit cost may vary; use the current tariff when solving problems.
Key terms
  • Power rating (P) – rate of energy conversion, measured in watts (W).
  • Energy (E) – amount of energy used; \(E = P t\).
  • kWh – kilowatt‑hour, equal to 3.6 MJ.
  • Energy efficiency – ratio of useful output energy to input energy.
  • Running cost – energy consumed multiplied by tariff.
