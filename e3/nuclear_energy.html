Nuclear fission

Nuclear fission is the process in which a heavy nucleus (such as 235U) splits into two lighter nuclei when struck by a neutron. The mass of the products is slightly less than the mass of the original nucleus; the mass difference \(\Delta m\) is converted into energy according to \(E=\Delta m c^2\). Because binding energy per nucleon peaks around medium‑mass nuclei, splitting heavy nuclei releases energy while fusing light nuclei (like hydrogen) also releases energy.【912087049771804†L5249-L5251】

In a chain reaction, neutrons produced by fission trigger further fissions. Controlling the rate of this reaction is essential for safe and steady power output.

Fission reactor design

A nuclear reactor uses the energy released from fission to produce heat, which then generates electricity in a conventional steam cycle. Core components include the fuel, a moderator to slow neutrons, control rods to absorb neutrons and adjust reactivity, and a coolant to transfer heat from the core to a steam generator.

Roles of moderator, coolant & control rods

  • Moderator: slows down fast neutrons emitted during fission to thermal energies, increasing the probability of further fission. Common moderators include water, heavy water and graphite.
  • Coolant: removes heat from the reactor core and transports it to a steam generator or directly to turbines. Water, liquid sodium or gas (CO2, helium) are used depending on reactor type.
  • Control rods: contain neutron‑absorbing materials such as boron or cadmium. Inserting control rods deeper into the core absorbs more neutrons and slows the chain reaction; withdrawing them increases power.
Common trap: Don’t confuse the moderator with the coolant. The moderator slows neutrons; the coolant transfers heat.

Reminders

Remember: Nuclear energy offers high energy density and low carbon emissions but raises concerns about radioactive waste, safety and proliferation. Understanding reactor components helps inform these debates.
