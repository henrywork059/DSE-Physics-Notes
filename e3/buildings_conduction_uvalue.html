Conduction basics

Heat flows through a solid wall by conduction. When one side of a wall is at temperature \(T_{\text{hot}}\) and the other at \(T_{\text{cold}}\) with \(T_{\text{hot}} > T_{\text{cold}}\), energy flows from the hot side to the cold side. The rate of energy transfer by conduction depends on the wall’s thermal conductivity \(\kappa\), its cross‑sectional area \(A\), its thickness \(d\) and the temperature difference:

\[\frac{dQ}{dt} = \kappa A \frac{T_{\text{hot}} - T_{\text{cold}}}{d}\,. \]

This equation shows that a wall with a larger area or higher thermal conductivity transfers heat faster, while a thicker wall reduces heat transfer. The HKDSE curriculum expresses this relation and asks students to discuss heat loss in conduction【912087049771804†L5174-L5178】.

Note: The factor \(\kappa\) (kappa) is a property of the material. Good conductors (like metals) have high \(\kappa\); insulating materials (such as foam or fiberglass) have low \(\kappa\).

Thermal transmittance (U‑value)

The U‑value of a building material is defined as the rate of heat flow through 1 m2 of the material per degree of temperature difference between its two sides. Mathematically it is the thermal conductivity divided by the thickness: \[U = \frac{\kappa}{d}\,. \] The unit of U‑value is W m−2 K−1. A smaller U‑value means better insulation. Students should be able to define the U‑value and solve related problems【912087049771804†L5178-L5184】.

For example, if a brick wall has \(\kappa = 0.80\;\text{W m}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}\) and thickness \(d=0.20\;\text{m}\), its U‑value is \(U = 0.80/0.20 = 4.0\;\text{W m}^{-2}\text{K}^{-1}\). This means that for every 1 °C temperature difference across 1 m2, 4 W of heat will pass through the wall.

Common trap: Don’t confuse thermal conductivity \(\kappa\) with U‑value. \(\kappa\) is an intrinsic property of the material, while the U‑value also depends on the wall thickness.

Worked example

A house wall has an area of 10 m2 and is built from a material with \(\kappa = 0.50\;\text{W m}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}\). The wall is 0.15 m thick. If the inside temperature is 24 °C and the outside temperature is 10 °C, estimate the rate of heat loss by conduction through the wall.

First compute the temperature difference \(\Delta T = 24 - 10 = 14\;°C\). The cross‑sectional area is \(A = 10\;\text{m}^2\). Applying the conduction formula gives

\[\frac{dQ}{dt} = \kappa A \frac{\Delta T}{d} = 0.50 \times 10 \times \frac{14}{0.15} \approx 466.7\;\text{W}\,. \]

The heat loss is therefore about 0.47 kW. Reducing the U‑value (e.g. by adding insulation or increasing wall thickness) will lower this loss.

Reminders

Remember: U‑values add like conductances. When several layers (e.g. brick + insulation + plaster) are in series, the overall thermal resistance is the sum of individual resistances; the overall U‑value is the reciprocal of the total resistance.
