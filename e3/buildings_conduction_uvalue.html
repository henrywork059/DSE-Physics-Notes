E3 — Buildings: Conduction & U-value

HKDSE Physics — Elective E3: Energy & Use of Energy · Use \(\dfrac{Q}{t}=\kappa A\dfrac{(T_{\text{hot}}-T_{\text{cold}})}{d}\) and \(U=\dfrac{\kappa}{d}\) to analyse heat transfer through building envelopes.

← Back to index ← Previous Next →

Two key tools in building questions: (1) conduction through a wall: \(\dfrac{Q}{t}=\kappa A\dfrac{\Delta T}{d}\), and (2) thermal transmittance (U-value): \(U=\dfrac{\kappa}{d}\) and \(\dfrac{Q}{t}=UA\Delta T\). Lower U means less heat transfer by conduction.

Exam focus: \(\dfrac{Q}{t}=\kappa A\dfrac{\Delta T}{d}\), \(U=\dfrac{\kappa}{d}\), \(\dfrac{Q}{t}=UA\Delta T\), unit: W and W m\(^{-2}\) K\(^{-1}\) Skills: pick \(A, d, \Delta T\); convert cm→m; interpret “lower U = better insulator”

1. Syllabus focus (what you must be able to do)

  • Interpret \(\dfrac{Q}{t}=\kappa A\dfrac{(T_{\text{hot}}-T_{\text{cold}})}{d}\) as the rate of energy transfer by conduction through a wall/roof/window.
  • Define thermal transmittance (U-value) of building materials as \(U=\dfrac{\kappa}{d}\) and solve related problems.
  • Use \(\dfrac{Q}{t}=UA(T_{\text{hot}}-T_{\text{cold}})\) to calculate heat transfer rate.
  • Explain qualitatively: lower U-value → better thermal insulator (less heat transfer by conduction).
Context
In building questions, heat flows through the building envelope (walls/roof/windows). For conduction calculations, the temperatures used are often the temperatures of the two surfaces of the envelope.

2. Rate of heat transfer by conduction (single layer)

Formula (plane wall)

  • \(\displaystyle \frac{Q}{t}=\kappa A\frac{(T_{\text{hot}}-T_{\text{cold}})}{d}\)
  • \(\kappa\): thermal conductivity (unit: W m\(^{-1}\) K\(^{-1}\))
  • \(A\): area of the wall/roof/window (m\(^2\))
  • \(d\): thickness (m)
  • \(\Delta T = T_{\text{hot}}-T_{\text{cold}}\): temperature difference across the material (K or °C)
Model: conduction through a wall Heat flows from hot to cold
What increases \(\dfrac{Q}{t}\)?
larger \(A\), larger \(\Delta T\), larger \(\kappa\)
What decreases \(\dfrac{Q}{t}\)?
larger thickness \(d\) (more insulation)
Common trap
Thickness is often given in cm. Convert to m before using the formula. (e.g. 10 cm = 0.10 m)

3. Thermal transmittance (U-value)

Definition (single material)

  • For a building envelope made of a single material with thickness \(d\) and thermal conductivity \(\kappa\):
  • \(\displaystyle U=\frac{\kappa}{d}\)
  • Unit of U-value: \(\text{W m}^{-2}\,\text{K}^{-1}\)

Using U-value to find heat transfer rate

  • \(\displaystyle \frac{Q}{t}=UA(T_{\text{hot}}-T_{\text{cold}}) = UA\Delta T\)
  • This is the same conduction model, but written using \(U\) instead of \(\kappa\) and \(d\).
Meaning
U-value can be interpreted as the overall rate of heat transfer through a unit area, divided by the temperature difference across the envelope.
Key idea
Lower U → lower \(\dfrac{Q}{t}\) for the same \(A\) and \(\Delta T\). So a material/component with a lower U-value is a better thermal insulator.

4. Typical U-values (for comparison)

U-values depend on both material and thickness. These values are used to compare insulation performance.

Component of building envelope U-value / W m\(^{-2}\) K\(^{-1}\)
Wall — Concrete wall (20 cm thick)5.5
Brick wall (20 cm thick)2.6
Brick wall (30 cm thick)2.0
Insulated wall (mineral wool between bricks)0.3
Window — Single-glazed window5.6
Double-glazed window (12 mm gap with air)2.8
Triple-glazed window (12 mm gap with air)1.9
How to use
If \(A\) and \(\Delta T\) are the same, then the component with the lower U-value has the lower heat gain/loss rate by conduction.

5. Worked examples

Example A: use \(\kappa\), \(A\), \(d\)

Concrete wall (air-conditioned room) Find \(\dfrac{Q}{t}\)
Given
\(\kappa=1.10\,\text{W m}^{-1}\,\text{K}^{-1}\), \(A=9\,\text{m}^2\), \(d=0.10\,\text{m}\), \(T_{\text{hot}}=33^\circ\text{C}\), \(T_{\text{cold}}=25^\circ\text{C}\)
Step
\(\Delta T=33-25=8\,\text{K}\)
Compute
\(\dfrac{Q}{t}=\kappa A\dfrac{\Delta T}{d}=1.10\times 9\times\dfrac{8}{0.10}=792\,\text{W}\)
Answer
\(\boxed{\dfrac{Q}{t}=792\,\text{W}}\)

Example B: use U-value

Insulating wall (U-value given) Find \(\dfrac{Q}{t}\)
Given
Wall size \(6\,\text{m}\times 3\,\text{m}\Rightarrow A=18\,\text{m}^2\), \(U=0.61\,\text{W m}^{-2}\,\text{K}^{-1}\), temperatures: 31°C and 23°C
Step
\(\Delta T=31-23=8\,\text{K}\)
Compute
\(\dfrac{Q}{t}=UA\Delta T=0.61\times 18\times 8=87.8\,\text{W}\)
Answer
\(\boxed{\dfrac{Q}{t}\approx 87.8\,\text{W}}\)
Direction vs magnitude
The formula gives the magnitude of heat transfer rate. If \(T_{\text{hot}}>T_{\text{cold}}\), heat flows from hot to cold.

6. Improving energy efficiency (reduce conduction heat gain/loss)

  • Use walls/roofs with thermal insulation (smaller \(\kappa\) or larger thickness \(d\) → smaller \(U\)).
  • Use double-glazed / triple-glazed windows to reduce U-value (smaller conduction heat transfer).
  • Reduce the exposed area \(A\) of high-U components (e.g. large single-glazed windows) if the question asks for a suggestion.
Exam phrasing
“Use a material/component with a lower U-value so the rate of heat transfer by conduction \(\left(\dfrac{Q}{t}=UA\Delta T\right)\) is smaller.”

7. Reminders & common exam traps

  • Unit check: \(\kappa\) is W m\(^{-1}\) K\(^{-1}\), \(U\) is W m\(^{-2}\) K\(^{-1}\), \(\dfrac{Q}{t}\) is W.
  • Convert cm to m for thickness \(d\).
  • \(\Delta T\) can be in K or °C because temperature differences are the same in both units.
  • U-value is about conduction. If heat transfer through glass is mainly by radiation, a lower U-value may not greatly reduce heating. (Radiation control is covered in later sections.)
Surface temperature
Many questions give the temperatures of the two surfaces of the wall/window. Use these directly as \(T_{\text{hot}}\) and \(T_{\text{cold}}\) unless told otherwise.

8. Key terms

  • Rate of heat transfer \(\dfrac{Q}{t}\): energy transferred per unit time (unit: W).
  • Thermal conductivity \(\kappa\): a material property; larger \(\kappa\) means the material conducts heat more easily.
  • Thermal transmittance (U-value) \(U\): for a single material, \(U=\dfrac{\kappa}{d}\) (unit: W m\(^{-2}\) K\(^{-1}\)).
  • Building envelope: the outermost layer of a building (roof, walls and windows).
  • Thermal insulator: material/component with a low U-value (low conduction heat transfer).
