Definitions & examples

An energy source is called renewable if it is naturally replenished on a human timescale; examples include sunlight, wind, flowing water, biomass and geothermal heat. Non‑renewable sources, such as coal, oil, natural gas and uranium, exist in finite quantities and take millions of years to form. The HKDSE syllabus requires students to describe the characteristics of renewable and non‑renewable energy sources and give examples【912087049771804†L5223-L5225】.

Renewable energy reduces reliance on fossil fuels, diversifies supply and lowers greenhouse gas emissions. However, some forms (like biomass) may compete with food production or have other environmental impacts. Non‑renewable sources are currently dominant in global energy supply but are associated with pollution and finite reserves.

Solar constant

The solar constant is defined as the total electromagnetic radiation energy from the Sun arriving per unit area per unit time at the mean distance between Earth and Sun, measured outside the atmosphere【912087049771804†L5226-L5229】. Its value is approximately 1.36 kW m−2.

In practice, solar irradiance at ground level is lower due to atmospheric absorption and scattering. Students should be able to solve problems involving the solar constant【912087049771804†L5230-L5232】, for instance estimating the power received by a panel of area \(A\) inclined at an angle to the Sun.

Example: A 2 m2 solar collector oriented perpendicular to the Sun’s rays in space receives \(P = 1.36\times 2 = 2.72\;\text{kW}\). On Earth, atmospheric effects reduce this to about 1.0 kW.

Wind turbine power

The power available from a wind of speed \(v\) blowing through a turbine rotor of area \(A\) and air density \(\rho\) is derived from the kinetic energy of the air. The maximum mechanical power that can be extracted is given by

\[P_{\text{max}} = \tfrac{1}{2} \eta \rho A v^3\,,\]

where \(\eta\) is the efficiency of the turbine【912087049771804†L5233-L5243】. Students should know this expression and be able to solve problems involving wind speed, turbine size and efficiency.

Note: The factor \(\tfrac{1}{2}\) comes from the kinetic energy density of moving air. No turbine can capture all of the wind’s energy — the Betz limit restricts the maximum efficiency to about 59 %.

Hydroelectric power

Hydroelectric power stations convert gravitational potential energy of water stored behind a dam into electrical energy. Water released through the dam flows down through turbines, driving generators. The energy conversion chain is:

  • Potential energy: water at height \(h\) has potential energy \(E_p = m g h\).
  • Kinetic energy: as water falls, potential energy converts to kinetic energy.
  • Mechanical work: flowing water turns the turbine blades.
  • Electrical energy: the generator converts mechanical energy into electricity.

Students should be able to describe this energy conversion process and solve related problems【912087049771804†L5243-L5245】.

Reminders

Remember: Renewable energy resources are not uniformly distributed and may be intermittent. Diversifying energy supply and integrating storage technologies can mitigate variability.
