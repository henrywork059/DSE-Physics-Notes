Understanding electric hotplates, induction cookers and microwave ovens

Electric hotplates

An electric hotplate consists of a resistive heating element embedded in a metal plate. When current flows through the element it heats up by Joule heating. Heat is transferred to the cookware by conduction. Hotplates are simple and inexpensive but have relatively slow response and low efficiency because energy is lost to the surroundings. They convert about 65–70 % of the electrical energy into useful heat.

Induction cookers

Induction cookers use a high‑frequency alternating current to produce a rapidly changing magnetic field. This field induces eddy currents in ferromagnetic cookware, causing it to heat up directly. There is no heating element: the pot itself is the source of heat. Induction cooking offers fast response and high end‑use efficiency (around 85 %) because little heat is lost to the air【912087049771804†L5108-L5124】.

Note: Induction cookers require compatible cookware made of ferrous metals (e.g. cast iron or stainless steel with a magnetic base).

Microwave ovens

Microwave ovens generate electromagnetic waves at about 2.45 GHz. These waves interact with polar water molecules in food, causing them to rotate and produce heat. Microwaves heat the food directly, reducing heat loss to the surroundings. They are efficient for small quantities or reheating but may not brown food or cook evenly for large dishes. Typical end‑use efficiency is around 50–60 %【912087049771804†L5108-L5124】.

Common trap: Microwaves do not heat food “from the inside out.” They penetrate a few centimetres and heat regions containing water; the rest heats by conduction.

Efficiency & running cost

The power rating of a cooker determines how quickly it can deliver heat. To compare running costs, calculate the energy consumed (\(E = P t\)) and multiply by the tariff. The curriculum emphasises using power ratings to determine running cost and solving problems involving end‑use energy efficiency【912087049771804†L5119-L5124】.

For example, a 1.5 kW induction cooker operating for 0.5 h consumes \(1.5 \times 0.5 = 0.75\) kWh. At HK$1.3 per kWh the cost is HK$0.98. If the cooker’s efficiency is 85 %, the useful heat delivered is 0.6375 kWh.

Reminders & Key terms

Reminders:
  • Hotplates rely on resistive heating; induction cookers heat the pot directly; microwaves excite water molecules【912087049771804†L5108-L5124】.
  • Use the power rating and operating time to calculate running cost【912087049771804†L5119-L5124】.
  • End‑use efficiency accounts for how much of the input electrical energy becomes useful heat.
Key terms
  • Hotplate – resistive heating element.
  • Induction cooking – uses magnetic induction to heat cookware.
  • Microwave heating – heating via absorption of microwave energy by water molecules.
  • End‑use efficiency – useful output energy / input energy.
