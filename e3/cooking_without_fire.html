1. Syllabus focus (what you must be able to do)

  • Describe how electric hotplates, induction cookers and microwave ovens generate heat for cooking.
  • Discuss advantages and disadvantages of these cookers based on energy conversions and practical use.
  • Solve problems involving end-use energy efficiency of cookers.
  • Use the power rating to determine energy consumption and running cost.
Note
End-use energy efficiency \(\eta_{\text{end}}\) only describes how much of the electrical energy input becomes available for heating in the cooking process. The actual cooking time also depends on the cooking method, the food and the cookware.

2. Three common electric cookers (quick overview)

Electric hotplate — heating effect in resistance wire
Induction cooker — eddy currents in metal pot
Microwave oven — microwaves heat polar molecules

What exam questions often ask

  • “Where is heat mainly generated?” (in cooker / in pot base / inside food)
  • “What is the main process involved?” (heating effect / electromagnetic induction / microwaves)
  • Compare “which is more energy efficient?” and explain using energy losses.

3. Working principles (what to write in words)

(a) Electric hotplate

  • A current passes through a coil of resistance wire.
  • Electrical energy is converted into internal energy of the wire by the heating effect of current.
  • Heat is transferred to the pot mainly by conduction (and/or radiation depending on design).

(b) Induction cooker

  • A high-frequency a.c. passes through a coil under a non-metallic top plate, producing a fast-changing magnetic field.
  • By electromagnetic induction, eddy currents are induced in the metal base of the pot.
  • The eddy currents produce heating in the pot base, and heat is then transferred to food by conduction.
Cookware requirement
Induction cookers usually require pots with a ferromagnetic base (e.g. iron or suitable stainless steel) so the induction effect is strong.

(c) Microwave oven

  • A device (magnetron) produces microwaves (typically about 2.45 GHz).
  • The microwaves penetrate a few cm into food. The oscillating electric field makes polar molecules (especially water) flip rapidly.
  • Collisions increase the average kinetic energy of molecules, so the temperature of the food increases.

4. Where is heat mainly generated? (very common MCQ)

Cooker Where heat is mainly generated Key idea
Electric hotplate In the cooker (resistance wire / heating element) Heating effect occurs first in the wire, then heat is transferred to pot
Induction cooker In the base of the pot Eddy currents are induced in pot base and heat it directly
Microwave oven Inside the food (mainly water-containing parts) Microwaves heat polar molecules throughout a depth of a few cm
Quick answer style
Use “mainly generated” + one sentence reason: e.g. “In the pot base because eddy currents are induced in the metal base and produce heating.”

5. Energy conversions (compare the three cookers)

Summary table

Cooker Main conversion (useful path) Main losses (typical)
Electric hotplate Electrical energy \(\to\) internal energy of resistance wire \(\to\) internal energy of pot \(\to\) internal energy of food Heat lost from hot wire / top plate to surroundings; other parts of cooker heated
Induction cooker Electrical energy \(\to\) magnetic field energy \(\to\) electrical energy of eddy currents in pot \(\to\) internal energy of pot \(\to\) food Some heat lost in coil; heat lost from pot to surroundings
Microwave oven Electrical energy \(\to\) microwaves \(\to\) internal energy of food Not all electrical energy becomes microwaves; not all microwaves absorbed; energy to operate lamp/turntable/fan
Common trap
Don’t say “current flows from the cooker coil into the pot”. For induction cooking, the changing magnetic field induces eddy currents within the pot base.

6. End-use energy efficiency (why induction is often higher)

Definition (same as other E3 pages)

  • \(\displaystyle \eta_{\text{end}} = \frac{E_{\text{out}}}{E_{\text{in}}}\times 100\%\)
  • For cookers, \(E_{\text{out}}\) is usually the energy available to heat the food (often given or found using \(mc\Delta T\)).
  • \(E_{\text{in}}\) is the electrical energy supplied: \(E_{\text{in}} = Pt\).

Typical comparison (idea only)

  • Electric hotplate: the heating element gets very hot, so more heat is lost to surroundings.
  • Induction cooker: the coil does not get very hot; heat is produced mainly in the pot base, so less direct heat loss from the cooker body.
  • Microwave oven: energy is lost in generating microwaves and not all microwaves are absorbed by food.
Exam wording
When comparing efficiencies, refer to energy losses (what gets heated besides the food?) instead of just “it is more efficient”.

7. Worked examples (HKDSE-style)

Example A — heating time using a microwave oven

  • A microwave oven has input power \(P_{\text{in}} = 800\,\text{W}\).
  • It heats \(m=0.30\,\text{kg}\) of food from \(4^\circ\text{C}\) to \(50^\circ\text{C}\). Take \(c=3400\,\text{J kg}^{-1}\,{}^\circ\text{C}^{-1}\).
  • End-use energy efficiency is \(\eta_{\text{end}} = 55\%\) in this process. Find the time \(t\).

Solution

  • Useful energy output: \(E_{\text{out}} = mc\Delta T = 0.30\times 3400\times(50-4)=4.692\times 10^4\,\text{J}\).
  • Useful power output: \(P_{\text{out}} = \eta_{\text{end}}\,P_{\text{in}} = 0.55\times 800 = 440\,\text{W}\).
  • Time: \(t = \dfrac{E_{\text{out}}}{P_{\text{out}}} = \dfrac{4.692\times 10^4}{440} \approx 1.07\times 10^2\,\text{s}\).
  • Answer: \(\boxed{t \approx 107\,\text{s}}\)
What \(P\) means here
In \(t = \dfrac{E}{P}\), the \(P\) should be the useful power output transferred to heating the food, not necessarily the input power.

Example B — running cost from energy needed for cooking

  • Average useful energy needed for cooking per day: \(E_{\text{out}} = 5.4\,\text{MJ}\).
  • End-use energy efficiency of an electric hotplate: \(\eta_{\text{end}} = 72\%\).
  • Electricity price: \(\$1.1\) per kW h.

Solution

  • Energy input: \(E_{\text{in}} = \dfrac{E_{\text{out}}}{\eta_{\text{end}}} = \dfrac{5.4\times 10^6}{0.72} = 7.5\times 10^6\,\text{J}\).
  • Convert to kW h: \(1\,\text{kW h} = 3.6\times 10^6\,\text{J}\), so \(E = \dfrac{7.5\times 10^6}{3.6\times 10^6} = 2.08\,\text{kW h}\).
  • Cost per day: \(\text{Cost} = 1.1\times 2.08 \approx \$2.29\).
  • Answer: \(\boxed{\$2.29\text{ per day}}\)

8. Reminders & common exam traps

  • State where heat is generated: hotplate → cooker; induction → pot base; microwave → inside food.
  • Induction cooker top plate: it is an insulator, so eddy currents do not form there; it may still become hot due to heat transferred from the pot.
  • Microwave heating works well for water-containing food because water molecules are polar (they flip in an oscillating electric field).
  • Efficiency is not the only factor: actual cooking time depends on food, cookware and cooking method.
  • Running cost uses kW h: \(\text{Cost} = (\$ / \text{kW h})\times E(\text{kW h})\).
Safety (typical)
For microwave ovens: avoid using metal containers; do not heat tightly closed containers. Follow the appliance manual.

9. Key terms

  • Heating effect of current: electrical energy \(\to\) internal energy when current passes through resistance.
  • Electromagnetic induction: a changing magnetic field induces an e.m.f. and current in a conductor.
  • Eddy currents: circulating currents induced in a bulk conductor; they can produce heating.
  • Polar molecule: has a positive end and a negative end due to non-uniform charge distribution (e.g. water).
  • End-use energy efficiency \(\eta_{\text{end}}\): \(\dfrac{E_{\text{out}}}{E_{\text{in}}}\times 100\%\).
  • kW h: unit of energy used for electricity billing; \(1\,\text{kW h} = 3.6\times 10^6\,\text{J}\).
