1. Syllabus focus (what you must be able to do)

  • Describe an air-conditioner as a heat pump that transfers heat from a cooler room to a hotter outside.
  • State the meaning of cooling capacity and use it in calculations.
  • Define and use the coefficient of performance (COP).
  • Discuss possible ways of using heat generated by central air-conditioning systems (energy saving).
Note
In DSE questions, you often read an energy label (cooling capacity in kW, annual energy consumption in kW h) and then find COP.

2. Heat pump idea: moving heat against its natural direction

  • Heat naturally flows from hot to cold.
  • A heat pump transfers heat from a colder place to a hotter place by consuming electrical energy.
  • An air-conditioner is a heat pump: it removes heat from indoor air and releases it outdoors.

How the room is cooled (exam-style)
In the evaporator coil, the refrigerant absorbs heat from indoor air and evaporates. The refrigerant is then compressed so its temperature becomes higher than the outside, and it releases heat in the condensing coil outdoors.

3. Cooling capacity (rate of heat removed)

  • Meaning: the rate at which an air-conditioner removes heat from a room.
  • Definition: \(\text{cooling capacity} = \dfrac{Q_C}{t}\).
  • Unit: W or kW (since it is a rate of energy transfer).
Unit trap
Cooling capacity is in kW (a rate), while annual energy consumption on labels is in kW h (energy).

4. Coefficient of performance (COP)

Meaning

  • COP measures how efficiently an air-conditioner uses energy to remove heat from a room.
  • COP is a ratio, so it has no unit.

Definition (cooling mode)

  • \(\displaystyle \text{COP} = \frac{\text{cooling capacity}}{P_{\text{in}}} = \frac{Q_C/t}{E_{\text{in}}/t} = \frac{Q_C}{E_{\text{in}}}\)
  • Typical air-conditioners may have COP around 2.3 to 3.2 (so each 1 kW h input can remove 2.3–3.2 kW h of heat from the room).
Cooling capacity vs COP (don’t mix them up) Capacity = how much; COP = how efficient
Quantity What it tells you Equation Unit
Cooling capacity maximum rate of heat removal \(\dfrac{Q_C}{t}\) W or kW
COP how efficiently input power removes heat \(\dfrac{\text{cooling capacity}}{P_{\text{in}}}\) no unit

5. Energy relation & “COP > 1” (does it break energy conservation?)

Rates form (most useful in questions)

  • \(\displaystyle \dot Q_{\text{out}} = \text{cooling capacity} + P_{\text{in}}\)
  • Meaning: the rate of heat released to outside equals the rate of heat removed from the room plus the electrical power input.

Energy form (over a time \(t\))

  • \(\displaystyle Q_{\text{out}} = Q_C + E_{\text{in}}\)
Why COP can be > 1
An air-conditioner does not “create” energy. It uses electrical energy mainly to operate the compressor, which allows it to move existing heat from the room to outside. So \(Q_C\) can be larger than \(E_{\text{in}}\), and COP can be larger than 1.
Common mistake
If the question asks for the heat released to outside, you must add the input power: \(\dot Q_{\text{out}} = \text{cooling capacity}+P_{\text{in}}\).

6. Worked examples (DSE style)

Example A: COP → heat removed and heat released

  • An air-conditioner has input power \(P_{\text{in}}=1500\,\text{W}\) and COP \(=2.3\).
  • (a) Cooling capacity \(= P_{\text{in}}\times \text{COP} = 1500\times2.3 = 3450\,\text{W}\).
  • Heat removed in 1 minute: \(Q_C = (3450)\times 60 = 2.07\times 10^5\,\text{J}\).
  • (b) Rate of heat released outside: \(\dot Q_{\text{out}} = 3450 + 1500 = 4950\,\text{W}\).

Example B: energy label → COP

  • Cooling capacity on label: \(5.12\,\text{kW}\). Annual energy consumption: \(2074\,\text{kW h}\) based on \(1200\,\text{h}\) operation.
  • Average input power: \(P_{\text{in}} = \dfrac{E}{t} = \dfrac{2074}{1200} = 1.73\,\text{kW}\).
  • \(\text{COP} = \dfrac{5.12}{1.73} = 2.96\).

Example C: monthly electricity cost → total heat removed

  • Electricity cost in a month: \(\$264\). Tariff: \(\$1.1\) per kW h. COP \(=2.5\).
  • Electrical energy input: \(E_{\text{in}} = \dfrac{264}{1.1} = 240\,\text{kW h}\).
  • Total heat removed from room: \(Q_C = \text{COP}\times E_{\text{in}} = 2.5\times240 = 600\,\text{kW h}\).
Quick meaning check
If COP = 3 (cooling), it means: the air-conditioner consumes 1 J of electrical energy to remove 3 J of heat from the room.

7. Energy saving ideas (what to write in exams)

Choosing and using an air-conditioner

  • Choose an air-conditioner with a suitable cooling capacity for the room (too small → runs longer; too large → unnecessary consumption).
  • For the same cooling capacity, choose a higher COP model → less electrical energy input for the same cooling effect.
  • Reduce heat gain into the room (e.g. close doors/windows; use curtains/solar-control film) → less heat to remove.

Using “waste heat” from central air-conditioning systems

  • Central air-conditioning ejects a large amount of heat to the environment.
  • If that heat is used for another purpose (e.g. heating water), overall energy use can be reduced.
Exam wording tip
Mention the reason: “Higher COP means less electrical energy input is needed to remove the same amount of heat.”

8. Reminders & common exam traps

  • COP has no unit. Don’t write “COP = 3 W”.
  • Don’t confuse cooling capacity (kW) with annual energy consumption (kW h).
  • When finding COP from a label: compute \(P_{\text{in}}\) first using \(P=\dfrac{E}{t}\).
  • Cooling capacity can be larger than input power, and COP can be > 1 (it does not violate energy conservation).
  • If asked for heat released to outside, use \(\dot Q_{\text{out}}=\text{cooling capacity}+P_{\text{in}}\).
Horsepower ≠ cooling capacity
Some air-conditioners are sold by “horsepower”. This refers to the power input, not the cooling capacity.

9. Key terms

  • Heat pump: device that transfers heat from cold to hot by consuming electrical energy.
  • Air-conditioner: a heat pump that removes heat from a room and releases it outdoors.
  • Cooling capacity: rate of heat removal from a room, \(\dfrac{Q_C}{t}\) (unit: kW).
  • COP (cooling): \(\dfrac{\text{cooling capacity}}{P_{\text{in}}}\), a measure of energy efficiency for removing heat (no unit).
  • Evaporator coil: indoor coil where refrigerant absorbs heat and evaporates.
  • Condensing coil: outdoor coil where refrigerant releases heat and condenses.
  • Compressor: increases pressure of refrigerant; requires electrical power input.
  • Expansion valve: lowers pressure; refrigerant cools down before entering the evaporator coil.
