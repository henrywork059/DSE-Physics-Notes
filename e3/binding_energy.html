Definition & mass defect

The binding energy of a nucleus is the energy required to separate all of its nucleons (protons and neutrons) to infinity. Equivalently, it is the energy released when a nucleus is assembled from its constituent nucleons. Due to mass–energy equivalence, the mass of a nucleus is less than the sum of the masses of its free nucleons; this mass defect \(\Delta m\) corresponds to the binding energy via \(E=\Delta m c^2\).

The binding energy per nucleon is the binding energy divided by the number of nucleons and is a useful measure of nuclear stability. The HKDSE syllabus explicitly requires students to define binding energy per nucleon and solve related problems【912087049771804†L5247-L5249】.

Example: The mass of a helium‑4 nucleus is 4.001506 u whereas two protons and two neutrons have a combined mass of 4.031880 u. The mass defect is 0.030374 u, corresponding to a binding energy of 28.3 MeV or 7.1 MeV per nucleon.

Binding energy curve

Plotting binding energy per nucleon against mass number yields the binding energy curve. It rises rapidly for light nuclei, peaks around iron (A ≈ 56) and then slowly declines for heavier nuclei. Nuclei near the peak are the most stable.

The shape of this curve explains why energy is released in both fusion and fission:

  • Fusion: Light nuclei (e.g. hydrogen isotopes) have low binding energy per nucleon. Fusing them into a heavier nucleus increases binding energy per nucleon, releasing energy.
  • Fission: Heavy nuclei (e.g. uranium, plutonium) have lower binding energy per nucleon than medium‑mass nuclei. Splitting them into intermediate nuclei increases the average binding energy per nucleon, releasing energy.

Students are expected to relate the binding energy curve to nuclear fission and fusion【912087049771804†L5250-L5251】.

Applications to fission & fusion

Understanding binding energy helps explain why certain nuclear reactions release vast amounts of energy:

  • Nuclear fission power: Splitting 235U or 239Pu in reactors produces about 200 MeV per fission. Chain reactions control the rate of energy release.
  • Thermonuclear fusion: Fusing deuterium and tritium yields a helium nucleus and a neutron, releasing about 17.6 MeV. Achieving the extreme temperatures and pressures required for sustained fusion is an ongoing scientific challenge.

Reminders

Remember: Binding energy per nucleon provides a measure of nuclear stability. Nuclei with very low binding energy per nucleon (e.g. hydrogen) or very high mass numbers (e.g. uranium) can release energy through fusion or fission respectively.
