Definition of OTTV

The Overall Thermal Transfer Value (OTTV) is defined as the average rate of heat gain per unit area into a building through the building envelope under specified conditions. It provides a single number that reflects how much heat enters a building due to conduction through walls and roofs, solar gains through windows and radiation.

According to the HKDSE syllabus, students should be able to define OTTV and solve related problems【912087049771804†L5186-L5189】. OTTV is often expressed in units of W m−2. A lower OTTV indicates a better thermally insulated building.

Note: OTTV consolidates various heat gain paths into one metric. It does not represent an instantaneous heat flow through a specific component but an average over the whole envelope.

Factors affecting OTTV

Several factors contribute to the OTTV of a building【912087049771804†L5189-L5192】:

  • U‑values of envelope components: Lower U‑values for walls, roofs and glazed areas reduce conduction heat gain.
  • Solar absorptance and reflectance: Darker surfaces absorb more solar radiation; light‑coloured or reflective surfaces reduce heat gain.
  • Window‑to‑wall ratio: Larger windows increase solar gain and therefore raise the OTTV. Using double glazing or low‑emissivity coatings helps.
  • Orientation and shading: The amount of direct sunlight striking a façade depends on its orientation. Shading devices, overhangs and vegetation can lower solar gains.
  • Solar control window films: Special coatings on glass selectively transmit visible light while rejecting infrared radiation, reducing heat gain【912087049771804†L5190-L5192】.
Common trap: Students sometimes think that reducing windows always lowers OTTV. In practice, daylighting saves artificial lighting energy; the optimal window size balances lighting and cooling loads.

Worked example

A commercial building has an envelope area of 2000 m2 with an average U‑value of 1.5 W m−2 K−1. Assume a typical temperature difference of 8 °C between indoors and outdoors, and that solar and air‑infiltration gains contribute an additional 40 % to the total heat gain. Estimate the OTTV.

First compute the conduction heat gain: \[Q_{\text{cond}} = U A \Delta T = 1.5 \times 2000 \times 8 = 24\,000\;\text{W}.\] Including an additional 40 % to account for solar and infiltration gains yields a total heat gain of \[Q_{\text{total}} = 1.40 \times 24\,000 = 33\,600\;\text{W}.\] The OTTV is the heat gain per unit area: \[\text{OTTV} = \frac{Q_{\text{total}}}{A} = \frac{33\,600}{2000} = 16.8\;\text{W m}^{-2}.\]

To reduce OTTV, one could decrease U‑values by adding insulation, install solar control glass or shading devices, and optimise window‑to‑wall ratio.

Reminders

Remember: OTTV is a design performance index used in building codes to limit heat gains. It is not a substitute for detailed heat load calculations but provides a quick way to assess envelope quality.
