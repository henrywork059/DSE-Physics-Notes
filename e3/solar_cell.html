Structure & working principle

A photovoltaic (PV) solar cell is essentially a p–n junction diode. The junction is formed by placing p‑type (excess holes) and n‑type (excess electrons) semiconductor materials in contact.

When sunlight is absorbed, photons with energy greater than the band gap excite electrons from the valence band to the conduction band, creating electron–hole pairs. The built‑in electric field at the p–n junction separates these charges: electrons are driven into the n‑region and holes into the p‑region. By connecting an external circuit, electrons flow through the load and recombine with holes on the other side, producing electrical power. Note: The p–n junction acts like a diode in the dark. Under illumination, it acts as a current source in parallel with a diode.

I–V characteristics

The current–voltage (I–V) curve of a solar cell shows how current varies with the voltage across the cell. Key points include:

  • Short‑circuit current \(I_{\text{SC}}\): the maximum current when the cell is short‑circuited (V = 0).
  • Open‑circuit voltage \(V_{\text{OC}}\): the voltage across the cell when no current flows (I = 0).
  • Maximum power point (MPP): the point on the I–V curve where the product \(I \times V\) is maximum. The fill factor (FF) is defined as \(\text{FF} = \frac{I_{\text{MP}} V_{\text{MP}}}{I_{\text{SC}} V_{\text{OC}}}\), a measure of how “square” the I–V curve is.

Practical cells include series and shunt resistances which reduce FF. The I–V curve shifts with illumination intensity and cell temperature.

Efficiency & applications

The energy conversion efficiency of a solar cell is the ratio of electrical power output at the MPP to the incident light power. Laboratory cells have achieved efficiencies over 25 %; commercial silicon modules typically range from 15 % to 22 %. Factors affecting efficiency include material quality, surface reflection, recombination losses and series resistance.

Applications of solar cells range from small calculators and road signs to large‑scale photovoltaic farms. Integrating solar panels on rooftops and facades contributes to a diversified and sustainable energy mix.

Reminders

Remember: Solar cells have no moving parts and require little maintenance but produce DC. In grid‑connected systems, an inverter converts the DC to AC. Matching the load to the maximum power point maximises efficiency.
