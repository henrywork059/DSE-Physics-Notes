Extraction and conversion

Obtaining energy resources and converting them into useful forms have environmental consequences. Mining fossil fuels and uranium can disturb ecosystems, contaminate water and produce toxic waste. Drilling and fracking may cause habitat loss and pollution. Refining and combusting fuels emit particulates, sulphur dioxide and nitrogen oxides, leading to air quality issues and acid rain.

On the other hand, renewable energy sources such as wind, solar and hydro generally have smaller life‑cycle impacts, though manufacturing solar panels and batteries still requires materials extraction and energy.

Consumption and society

Energy use affects society beyond the environment. Dependence on imported fuels influences energy security and geopolitics. High electricity prices can widen social inequalities. Land occupied by power plants, transmission lines and fuel transport infrastructure competes with other uses and may displace communities.

The HKDSE syllabus asks students to discuss the impact of extraction, conversion, distribution and use of energy on the environment and society【912087049771804†L5258-L5260】.

Greenhouse gases & global warming

Carbon dioxide (CO2), methane (CH4) and nitrous oxide (N2O) trap heat in the Earth’s atmosphere by absorbing infrared radiation. These greenhouse gases are released primarily through the burning of fossil fuels, agriculture and industrial processes. The resulting enhanced greenhouse effect leads to global warming, sea‑level rise and extreme weather patterns.

The syllabus emphasises discussing the effect of greenhouse gases on global warming【912087049771804†L5261-L5262】 and encourages students to think critically about mitigation strategies such as energy efficiency, renewable energy and carbon capture.

Analysing fuel consumption data

Analysing consumption data helps identify which fuels are used most and for what purposes. In Hong Kong, for example, electricity is generated mainly from natural gas and imported nuclear power, while transport relies heavily on petroleum products. The curriculum encourages students to analyse consumption data for different fuel types and their specific purposes【912087049771804†L5264-L5265】.

Such analysis supports policy decisions, such as switching from coal to gas for electricity generation, promoting public transport and incentivising renewable energy adoption.

Reminders

Remember: Energy choices have far‑reaching consequences. Consider environmental impact alongside cost, reliability and social equity when evaluating different options.
