Electric vehicles (EVs)

In a pure battery electric vehicle, chemical energy stored in a high‑capacity battery is converted into mechanical energy by an electric motor. The main components of the power system are:

  • Battery pack: stores electrical energy. Lithium‑ion cells are commonly used for their high energy density.
  • Power electronics: an inverter converts DC from the battery into AC to drive the motor; a converter provides auxiliary voltages for lights and accessories.
  • Electric motor: converts electrical energy to mechanical torque to propel the vehicle. Regenerative braking allows the motor to act as a generator during deceleration.

EVs produce no tailpipe emissions and convert a larger fraction of stored energy into motion compared to internal combustion engines. Students should be able to state the main components of EV power systems【912087049771804†L5203-L5206】.

Note: The overall environmental benefit of EVs depends on how the electricity is generated. Renewable sources yield the greatest emission reductions.

Hybrid vehicles

Hybrid vehicles combine a conventional internal combustion engine with one or more electric motors. The key components include:

  • Engine: usually petrol‑powered, smaller than in a conventional vehicle.
  • Electric motor/generator: assists the engine and regenerates energy during braking.
  • Battery: typically of lower capacity than in a pure EV, charged by the engine and through regenerative braking.
  • Power split device: (e.g. planetary gear or electronically controlled CVT) blends power from the engine and motor.

The HKDSE syllabus requires students to state the main components of hybrid vehicles and compare their end‑use energy efficiency to fossil‑fuel vehicles【912087049771804†L5208-L5210】.

Comparison with conventional vehicles

Electric motors are inherently more efficient (around 85 – 90 %) than internal combustion engines (25 – 35 %). Hybrids improve efficiency by recovering braking energy and operating the engine closer to its optimal efficiency point. As a result, hybrids and EVs have lower fuel or energy costs per kilometre and produce fewer emissions.

However, battery weight, limited range and charging infrastructure are practical challenges. When comparing end‑use energy efficiency, consider the entire energy chain — from fuel extraction to wheel motion — not just the vehicle itself.

Reminders

Remember: Hybrids are a transitional technology. They combine advantages of EVs and conventional vehicles but still rely on fossil fuels. Understanding the components helps compare their efficiency and environmental impact.
