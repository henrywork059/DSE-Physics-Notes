E3 — Lighting: Luminous Flux (lm)

HKDSE Physics — Elective E3 (Lighting) · Luminous flux \(\Phi\) measures how bright a light source appears to the human eye.

← Back to index ← Previous Next →

In DSE lighting questions, don’t use watt to judge “brightness”. Use luminous flux (unit: lumen, lm). A source with a larger \(\Phi\) usually looks brighter.

1. Syllabus focus (what you must be able to do)

  • Define luminous flux \(\Phi\) as the power output of a light source corrected for human vision.
  • Use lumen (lm) as the unit of luminous flux.
  • State that eye response depends on wavelength (most sensitive near 555 nm).
  • Compare the brightness of light sources using \(\Phi\) (not just power rating in W).
Note
Two light sources with the same luminous flux look equally bright to us, even if their colours are different.

2. What is luminous flux \(\Phi\)?

  • Luminous flux describes how bright a light source appears to the human eye.
  • It is the power emitted by a light source with correction for the sensitivity of the human eye.
  • Symbol: \(\Phi\)
  • Unit: lumen (lm)
Common trap
Watt (W) is the rate of energy transfer (power). Lumen (lm) is not the same as W. A lamp with larger power is not necessarily brighter.
Exam wording
You can write: “Luminous flux is the power output of a light source corrected for the dependence on wavelength of the sensitivity of the human eye. Unit: lm.”

3. Eye response depends on wavelength (peak at 555 nm)

  • The human eye is most sensitive to green-yellow light of wavelength about 555 nm.
  • For the same physical power of visible light, green-yellow light usually looks brighter than blue or red.
Qualitative eye sensitivity curve Peak ≈ 555 nm
Key idea
Luminous flux includes a correction for eye response, so it matches how bright the light looks to us.

4. How to use \(\Phi\) in DSE questions

(A) Compare brightness

  • If \(\Phi_1 > \(\Phi_2\), source 1 usually looks brighter.
  • If \(\Phi_1 = \(\Phi_2\), they look equally bright to our eyes (even if colours differ).

(B) Link with efficacy (connection to the next pages)

  • For many lamps, packaging/labels give luminous efficacy (unit: lm W\(^{-1}\)).
  • Relationship: \(\text{efficacy} = \dfrac{\Phi}{P}\) \(\Rightarrow\) \(\Phi = (\text{efficacy})\times P\).
Be careful
Don’t say “higher wattage = brighter”. Always check \(\Phi\) (lm), or compare efficacy \(\Phi/P\) when \(\Phi\) is not directly given.

5. Worked examples

Example 1 — Compare brightness using luminous flux

  • Lamp A: \(\Phi = 800\,\text{lm}\)
  • Lamp B: \(\Phi = 1200\,\text{lm}\)
  • Conclusion: Lamp B looks brighter (larger \(\Phi\)).

Example 2 — Same luminous flux, different colour

  • Two lamps emit different colours but both have \(\Phi = 880\,\text{lm}\).
  • Conclusion: They look equally bright to us (same \(\Phi\)).

Example 3 — Find luminous flux from efficacy and power

  • Given: efficacy \(= 110\,\text{lm W}^{-1}\), input power \(P = 6.5\,\text{W}\)
  • \(\Phi = (\text{efficacy})\times P = 110\times 6.5 = 715\,\text{lm}\)
  • Answer: \(\boxed{715\,\text{lm}}\)

In exam questions, you rarely need these numbers; you mainly use \(\Phi\) to compare brightness.

6. Reminders & common exam traps

  • Unit check: luminous flux is in lm, not W.
  • Eye response: for the same physical power, different colours can look different in brightness.
  • Brightness comparison: if \(\Phi\) is given, compare \(\Phi\) directly.
  • Connection: if \(\Phi\) is not given, the question may give efficacy (lm W\(^{-1}\)) and power to find \(\Phi\).
Exam sentence
“A light source consuming a large amount of energy is not necessarily bright. Luminous flux is more useful than power rating when choosing a lamp for illumination.”

7. Key terms

  • Luminous flux \(\Phi\): power emitted by a light source, corrected for the sensitivity of the human eye (unit: lm).
  • Lumen (lm): unit of luminous flux.
  • Eye response: the sensitivity of the human eye depends on wavelength; maximum near 555 nm.
  • (Link) Efficacy: \(\dfrac{\Phi}{P}\), measured in lm W\(^{-1}\) (details in the efficacy page).
