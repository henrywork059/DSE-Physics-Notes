Lighting: Efficacy

Luminous flux per unit electrical power

Definition Calculation Worked example Reminders

Definition

Luminous efficacy \(\eta\) is defined as the ratio of luminous flux to electrical power input【912087049771804†L5105-L5106】: \[\eta = \frac{\Phi}{P}\, .\] It measures how efficiently a light source converts electrical energy into visible light. The unit of efficacy is lumens per watt (lm W−1).

The higher the efficacy, the more light you obtain for the same power. For example, an LED lamp with \(\eta = 100\,\text{lm W}^{-1}\) produces roughly six times as much light as an incandescent bulb of \(\eta = 15\,\text{lm W}^{-1}\) for the same power input.

Calculation

Efficacy can be measured directly by dividing the measured luminous flux by the electrical power consumed:

\[\eta = \frac{\Phi}{P}\, .\]

Manufacturers often provide nominal efficacy values. When comparing light sources, remember that the quoted luminous flux is adjusted to the human eye’s sensitivity. A source emitting mostly infrared may have low luminous efficacy even if it radiates a lot of energy.

Worked example

A 12 W compact fluorescent lamp (CFL) produces 800 lm of light. A 60 W incandescent bulb produces 720 lm. Compare their efficacies and determine how much power an LED lamp would need to produce 800 lm if its efficacy is 100 lm W−1.

Solution:

  1. \(\eta_{\text{CFL}} = 800/12 \approx 66.7\) lm W−1.
  2. \(\eta_{\text{inc}} = 720/60 = 12\) lm W−1.
  3. An LED lamp producing 800 lm at 100 lm W−1 requires \(P = 800/100 = 8\) W.

Thus the CFL is over five times as efficient as the incandescent bulb, and the LED lamp is even more efficient.

Reminders & Key terms

Reminders:
  • Efficacy is the lumens per watt of input power【912087049771804†L5105-L5106】.
  • Compare lamps by both luminous flux and efficacy.
  • Be careful not to confuse efficacy (lm W−1) with efficiency (%).
Key terms
  • Luminous efficacy \(\eta\) – lumens per watt of electrical input.
  • Luminous flux \(\Phi\) – total visible light output (lm).
  • Electrical power \(P\) – rate of energy consumption (W).
