E3 — Illuminance (lux)

HKDSE Physics — Elective E3: Lighting · Define illuminance, use lux, and apply inverse-square law and Lambert’s cosine law in calculations.

← Back to index ← Previous Next →

Illuminance questions are mainly about how much light is received by a surface. Start from the definition \(E=\dfrac{\Phi}{A}\), then choose the correct case: parallel beam (area) or point source (distance \(r\)).

Exam focus: \(E=\Phi/A\), \(1\,\text{lx}=1\,\text{lm m}^{-2}\), inverse-square law, Lambert’s cosine law Skills: convert cm\(^2\) to m\(^2\), identify angle to the normal, choose correct distance \(r\)

1. Syllabus focus (what you must be able to do)

  • Define illuminance as luminous flux falling on unit area of a surface.
  • Use lux (lx) as the unit of illuminance.
  • Use inverse-square law and Lambert’s cosine law to solve illuminance problems.
Meaning
Illuminance describes how brightly a surface is illuminated (received light per unit area).

2. Definition & unit

Definition

  • Illuminance \(E\) is the luminous flux \(\Phi\) falling on a surface per unit area \(A\):
  • \(\displaystyle E = \frac{\Phi}{A}\)

Unit

  • Unit of illuminance: lux (lx)
  • \(\displaystyle 1\,\text{lx} = 1\,\text{lm m}^{-2}\)
Measurement
Illuminance can be measured using a light meter (unit: lx).
Quick unit check remember: lm over area
Formula
\(E=\Phi/A\)
So unit of \(E\)
\(\text{lx} = \text{lm m}^{-2}\)

3. Parallel light beam (area method)

Light falls perpendicularly

  • If a parallel beam with luminous flux \(\Phi\) illuminates area \(A_{\perp}\) (area normal to the beam), then
  • \(\displaystyle E_{\perp} = \frac{\Phi}{A_{\perp}}\)
Common trap
For a parallel light beam, illuminance does not depend on distance from the source (ignore inverse-square law).

4. Lambert’s cosine law (light at an angle)

Key idea

  • When light strikes a surface at an angle \(\theta\) to the normal, it spreads over a larger area, so illuminance decreases.

Formula

  • \(\displaystyle E = E_{\perp}\cos\theta\)
Angle definition (always to the normal) use \(\cos\theta\)

\(\theta\) is the angle between the incident light and the normal at the point.

Angle trap
Be careful: \(\theta\) is to the normal, not to the surface. If the question gives an angle to the surface, convert it first.

5. Point source: inverse-square law

Light falls perpendicularly on a spherical surface

  • A point source emits uniformly in all directions.
  • At distance \(r\), light spreads over spherical area \(4\pi r^2\).
  • So the illuminance on a surface perpendicular to the light is:
  • \(\displaystyle E_{\perp} = \frac{\Phi}{4\pi r^2}\)

Inverse-square law

  • For a point source: \(E_{\perp} \propto \dfrac{1}{r^2}\)

Point source + angle (combine with cosine law)

  • \(\displaystyle E = E_{\perp}\cos\theta = \frac{\Phi\cos\theta}{4\pi r^2}\)
Shortcut
If light is perpendicular at the point, then \(\theta=0\) and \(\cos\theta=1\). Use \(E=\Phi/(4\pi r^2)\).

6. Worked example (point source → illuminance)

Example A (perpendicular) use \(\Phi/(4\pi r^2)\)
Given
\(\Phi=7500\,\text{lm}\), \(r=2.0\,\text{m}\) (perpendicular)
Calculation
\(\displaystyle E=\frac{7500}{4\pi(2.0)^2}=\frac{7500}{16\pi}\approx 149\,\text{lx}\)

Final: \(\boxed{149\,\text{lx}}\) (3 s.f.)

Example B (at an angle) add \(\cos\theta\)
Given
\(\Phi=7500\,\text{lm}\), \(r=2.5\,\text{m}\), \(\cos\theta=\frac{2.0}{2.5}=0.80\)
Calculation
\(\displaystyle E=\frac{\Phi\cos\theta}{4\pi r^2}=\frac{7500\times 0.80}{4\pi(2.5)^2}\approx 76.4\,\text{lx}\)

Final: \(\boxed{76.4\,\text{lx}}\) (3 s.f.)

Geometry trap
In point-source questions, \(r\) is the straight-line distance from the source to the point (use Pythagoras if needed), and \(\cos\theta\) comes from the same triangle.

7. Reminders & common exam traps

  • Definition first: \(E=\Phi/A\). Lux is \(\text{lm m}^{-2}\).
  • Point source: use \(E_{\perp}=\Phi/(4\pi r^2)\) and inverse-square law.
  • Parallel beam: use area method \(E_{\perp}=\Phi/A_{\perp}\) (no inverse-square).
  • Angle: use Lambert’s cosine law \(E=E_{\perp}\cos\theta\), where \(\theta\) is to the normal.
  • Area units: \(1\,\text{cm}^2 = 10^{-4}\,\text{m}^2\). Convert before using \(E=\Phi/A\).
Real-life context
Typical places need different illuminance (e.g. reading/work area needs higher lux than a corridor). In exams, you mainly do calculations and comparisons.

8. Key terms

  • Illuminance \(E\): luminous flux received per unit area, \(E=\Phi/A\) (unit: lx).
  • Lux (lx): unit of illuminance, \(1\,\text{lx}=1\,\text{lm m}^{-2}\).
  • Luminous flux \(\Phi\): total light output (unit: lm).
  • Inverse-square law: for a point source, \(E_{\perp}\propto 1/r^2\).
  • Lambert’s cosine law: \(E=E_{\perp}\cos\theta\), \(\theta\) to the normal.
  • Normal: line perpendicular to a surface at a point.
