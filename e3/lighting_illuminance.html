Lighting: Illuminance

How light falls on a surface and the laws governing it

Definition

Illuminance \(E\) is the luminous flux falling on a unit area of a surface【912087049771804†L5097-L5104】. It measures how well a surface is illuminated. The SI unit of illuminance is the lux (lx), where \(1\,\text{lx} = 1\,\text{lm}/\text{m}^2\).

If a luminous flux \(\Phi\) uniformly illuminates an area \(A\), then \[E = \frac{\Phi}{A}\, .\] For example, a 200 lumen lamp evenly lighting a 2 m2 desk produces an illuminance of 100 lux.

Inverse square and cosine laws

When a point source of luminous intensity \(I\) (in candela) is at a distance \(r\) from a small surface, the illuminance is given by the inverse square law: \[E = \frac{I}{r^2}\, .\] This law states that illuminance decreases in proportion to the square of the distance from the source【912087049771804†L5097-L5104】.

If the surface is tilted relative to the incoming light, the effective area increases by a factor \(1/\cos\theta\), where \(\theta\) is the angle between the normal to the surface and the direction to the source. Combining this with the inverse square law yields Lambert’s cosine law: \[E = \frac{I \cos\theta}{r^2}\, .\]

Note: The cosine law accounts for the fact that a slanted surface intercepts less flux per unit area. When \(\theta = 0\) the surface faces the source directly and the illuminance is maximum.

Worked example

A reading lamp has a luminous intensity of 60 cd. It is placed 0.8 m above a horizontal desk. Find the illuminance on the desk (a) directly under the lamp and (b) at a point 0.6 m horizontally away from the point under the lamp.

Solution:

  1. (a) At the point directly under the lamp, \(r = 0.8\) m and \(\theta = 0\). Thus \(E = I/r^2 = 60/(0.8^2) \approx 93.8\) lx.
  2. (b) At the off‑axis point, the line from the lamp to the point has length \(r = \sqrt{0.8^2 + 0.6^2} \approx 1.0\) m. The angle \(\theta\) between the normal to the desk and the light ray is given by \(\cos\theta = 0.8/1.0 = 0.8\). Thus \(E = (60 \times 0.8)/(1.0^2) = 48\) lx.

Notice how the illuminance drops off when the point is both farther from the source and at an angle to it. These calculations are typical of those required in examination questions【912087049771804†L5097-L5104】.

Reminders & Key terms

Reminders:
  • Illuminance (lux) is flux per unit area【912087049771804†L5097-L5104】.
  • Use the inverse square law for point sources; include \(\cos\theta\) for tilted surfaces.
  • Don’t confuse illuminance (lux) with luminous flux (lumens).
Key terms
  • Illuminance \(E\) – luminous flux per unit area (lux).
  • Inverse square law – \(E \propto 1/r^2\).
  • Lambert’s cosine law – \(E = I \cos\theta / r^2\).
  • Luminous intensity \(I\) – luminous flux per unit solid angle (cd).
