1. Basic arrangement

  • Monochromatic light of frequency \( f \) shines on a metal cathode in vacuum.
  • The cathode and anode form a photocell connected to a microammeter and a variable d.c. power supply to measure the photocurrent for different applied potentials.
HKDSE Reminder
The use of a gold-leaf electroscope in the photoelectric effect experiment is not required. Applications of photocells are also not required (but using a photocell to study the effect is implied).

2. Main observations

▶ Launch Interactive Experiment
  • Threshold frequency \( f_0 \): for \( f < f_0 \), no photoelectrons are emitted, regardless of light intensity.
  • For \( f > f_0 \), emission of photoelectrons is almost instantaneous, even for very weak light.
  • At fixed \( f > f_0 \), increasing light intensity increases the photocurrent (number of electrons emitted per second).
  • For a given metal, the maximum kinetic energy (or stopping potential \( V_s \)) depends on \( f \), not on light intensity.

Comparison: wave model vs photon model

Experimental observation Prediction by classical wave model Explanation by photon model (Einstein)
No photoelectrons are emitted when \( f < f_0 \), no matter how intense the light is. Electrons should eventually gain enough energy to escape even at low frequency if the light is intense enough or shines for a long time. Each photon has energy \( hf \). If \( hf < \phi \), no electron can escape. Increasing intensity increases the number of photons, not the energy of each photon.
For \( f > f_0 \), emission is almost instantaneous, even for very weak light. At very low intensity, electrons should need time to accumulate enough energy before leaving the metal. A single photon can transfer its energy to one electron in one interaction, so emission occurs without time lag if \( hf \) is large enough.
For a given metal, the maximum kinetic energy (or \( V_s \)) depends on \( f \), not on intensity. Higher intensity should give more energy to each electron → larger maximum kinetic energy is expected. At fixed \( f \), photon energy \( hf \) is fixed. Intensity affects how many photons arrive each second, not the energy per photon.
At fixed \( f > f_0 \), photocurrent increases with light intensity. Higher intensity can drive more electrons away per second. Higher intensity means more photons per second striking the surface → more electrons emitted per second, so photocurrent increases.

3. Photon model of light

  • Light consists of particles called photons.
  • Energy of each photon: $$ E = hf $$
  • At a given \( f \), intensity is proportional to the number of photons incident per second per unit area. Higher intensity → more photons per second → larger photocurrent.
  • For a monochromatic beam of power \( P \) and frequency \( f \), number of photons per second is: $$ \frac{P}{hf} $$
HKDSE Reminder
You must be able to relate intensity (fixed \( f \)) to the number of photons. Millikan’s photoelectric experiment is not required.

4. Intensity of a torch (photon view)

Definition & Link
Definition: $$ \text{Intensity} = \frac{P}{A} $$
Photon link: energy delivered in time \( t \) is \( E = Nhf \), so:
$$ P = \frac{E}{t} = \frac{Nhf}{t} \quad \rightarrow \quad \text{Intensity} = \frac{P}{A} = \frac{Nhf}{At} $$
At fixed \( f \), higher intensity means larger \( \frac{N}{t} \) (more photons per second per unit area), not larger energy per photon.

5. Key terms

Photoelectric effect: emission of electrons from a metal surface when electromagnetic radiation shines on it.

Photon: a particle of light. Energy of each photon is \( E = hf \).

Photoelectron: an electron emitted from the metal surface due to the photoelectric effect.

Work function \( \phi \): minimum energy needed for an electron to escape from a metal surface.

Threshold frequency \( f_0 \): the minimum frequency needed to produce photoelectrons.

Stopping potential \( V_s \): the minimum retarding potential magnitude that just stops the most energetic photoelectrons so the photocurrent falls to zero.

Photocurrent: current in the external circuit due to the flow of photoelectrons.

Intensity: power per unit area, \( \text{Intensity} = P/A \). At fixed \( f \), higher intensity means more photons per second per unit area, not more energy per photon.

6. Common traps

Warning / Trap
  • Trap: “Higher intensity means each photon has higher energy.”
    Fix: photon energy depends on \( f \) only: \( E = hf \). Intensity changes the number of photons per second.
  • Trap: “At low intensity, there must be a time delay.”
    Fix: emission is (almost) instantaneous when \( f > f_0 \).
  • Trap: “If \( f < f_0 \), increasing intensity will eventually cause emission.”
    Fix: no emission occurs because \( hf \) is too small.
