1.1 Basic arrangement Setup

  • Monochromatic light of frequency f shines on a metal cathode in vacuum.
  • The cathode and anode form a photocell connected to a microammeter and a variable d.c. power supply to measure the photocurrent for different applied potentials.
HKDSE note: Use of gold-leaf electroscope and practical applications of photocells are not required in the syllabus. Focus on the basic photocell arrangement and its behaviour.

1.2 Main observations Evidence

  • Threshold frequency f0: for f < f0, no photoelectrons are emitted, regardless of light intensity.
  • For f > f0, emission of photoelectrons is almost instantaneous, even for very weak light.
  • At fixed f > f0, increasing light intensity increases the photocurrent (number of electrons emitted per second).
  • For a given metal, the maximum kinetic energy (or stopping potential) of the photoelectrons depends only on f, not on light intensity.

Comparison: wave model vs photon model

Experimental observation Prediction by classical wave model Explanation by photon model (Einstein)
No photoelectrons are emitted when f < f0, no matter how intense the light is. Electrons should eventually gain enough energy to escape even at low frequency if the light is intense enough or shines for a long time. Emission is expected for any frequency. Each photon has energy hf. If hf < φ, no electron can escape. Increasing intensity only increases the number of photons, not the energy of each photon.
For f > f0, emission is almost instantaneous, even for very weak light. At very low intensity, the wave carries little power, so surface electrons should need time to accumulate enough energy before leaving the metal. A single photon can give all its energy to one electron in one interaction, so emission occurs without time lag provided hf > φ.
For a given metal, the maximum kinetic energy (or stopping potential) depends only on f, not on intensity. Higher intensity (larger amplitude) should give more energy to each electron → larger maximum kinetic energy is expected. Kmax = hf − φ shows maximum energy depends only on frequency. Intensity changes the number of emitted electrons, not Kmax.
At fixed f > f0, photocurrent increases with light intensity. Higher intensity means a stronger wave, which can drive more electrons away from the metal per second. Higher intensity means more photons per second striking the surface → more electrons are emitted per second, so photocurrent increases.

1.3 Photon model of light Model

  • Light consists of particles called photons.
  • Energy of each photon: E = h f
  • At a given f, the intensity Int is proportional to the number of photons incident per second per unit area. Higher intensity → more photons per second → larger photocurrent.
  • For a monochromatic beam of power P and frequency f, number of photons per second is Ph f.

Intensity of a torch (photon view)

Definition: Int = PA
Photon link: energy delivered in time t is E = N h f, so
P = Et = N h ft  →  Int = PA = N h fA t
At fixed f, higher intensity means larger Nt (more photons per second per unit area), not larger energy per photon.

1.4 Einstein’s photoelectric equation Formula

Energy conservation for one photon hitting one surface electron:

h f = φ + 12 me vmax2 Kmax = 12 me vmax2 = h f - φ
  • φ (work function): minimum energy required to just remove an electron from the surface.
  • Photoelectrons are emitted with a range of kinetic energies from 0 up to Kmax.

Threshold frequency

At threshold, electrons just escape with zero kinetic energy:

φ = h f0

The corresponding threshold wavelength λ0 satisfies f0 = cλ0, so λ0 = cf0.

Stopping potential

When a retarding potential is just enough to stop the most energetic photoelectrons, its magnitude is the stopping potential Vs. (If the anode is made negative, at stopping: V = −Vs.)

e Vs = Kmax = h f - φ
Graph: Vs against f is a straight line with slope he and intercept −φe.
HKDSE note: You are not required to learn the detailed design/history of Millikan’s photoelectric experiment. However, using the Vs–f straight-line relationship to find f0, h, or φ is examinable.

Alternative form in terms of wavelength

Since c = fλ, Einstein’s equation can also be written as

Kmax = h f - φ = h cλ - φ

Therefore, a graph of Kmax against 1λ is a straight line with:

  • slope h c (same for all metals)
  • vertical intercept −φ
  • horizontal intercept at 1λ0, which gives the threshold wavelength λ0

Photocurrent versus applied potential (qualitative)

  • At V = 0, some photoelectrons still reach the anode → a non-zero photocurrent flows if f > f0.
  • As the anode is made more positive, more emitted electrons are collected and the photocurrent increases, then reaches a saturation value when all emitted electrons are collected.
  • If the anode is made negative, the potential retards the electrons. The photocurrent decreases and becomes zero when the retarding potential is just enough to stop the most energetic photoelectrons.
  • For fixed f > f0, increasing intensity increases the saturation photocurrent but Vs remains unchanged.
  • For fixed intensity, increasing f (above f0) increases Kmax and hence increases Vs, while the saturation photocurrent stays approximately the same.

1.5 Significance Why?

  • The photoelectric effect provides strong evidence that light has a particle nature (photon model).
  • It shows that energy exchange between light and matter occurs in discrete packets of size h f.

1.6 Key terms for HKDSE (Photoelectric effect) Glossary

Photoelectric effect: emission of electrons from the surface of a metal when electromagnetic radiation of sufficiently high frequency is incident on it.

Photon: a quantum (packet) of light energy with energy E = h f.

Photoelectron: an electron emitted from a metal surface as a result of the photoelectric effect.

Work function φ: the minimum energy required to just remove an electron from the surface of a metal. It can be expressed in J or eV.

φ = h f0

Threshold frequency f0: the minimum frequency that can just cause photoelectrons to be emitted. For f < f0, no photoelectrons are emitted no matter how intense the light is.

Stopping potential Vs: the smallest retarding potential magnitude that just stops the most energetic photoelectrons so that the photocurrent falls to zero.

Photocurrent: the electric current in the external circuit due to the motion of photoelectrons emitted from the cathode and collected by the anode.

HKDSE common traps & mistakes (Photoelectric effect)

  • Mixing up the roles of frequency and intensity: Kmax and Vs depend on f only (for f > f0), while photocurrent and saturation current depend mainly on intensity.
  • Not recognising from an I–V graph that the photocurrent reaches a saturation value at some positive potential.
  • Confusing maximum kinetic energy, work function and stopping potential as if they were the same quantity.
  • Using hf = eVs directly without subtracting the work function. Correct relation: eVs = hf - φ.
  • Forgetting that no photoelectrons are emitted when f < f0 (or λ > λ0), no matter how strong the light is.
  • Not converting properly between eV and J when substituting into equations.
  • Misreading a graph of Vs against f (or Kmax against 1λ): assuming the line must pass through the origin, confusing the slope, or not identifying the threshold intercept correctly.
