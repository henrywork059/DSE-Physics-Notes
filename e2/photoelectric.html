1. Basic arrangement

  • Monochromatic light of frequency f shines on a metal cathode in vacuum.
  • The cathode and anode form a photocell connected to a microammeter and a variable d.c. power supply to measure the photocurrent for different applied potentials.
HKDSE reminder: The use of a gold-leaf electroscope in the photoelectric effect experiment is not required. Applications of photocells are also not required (but using a photocell to study the effect is implied).

2. Main observations

  • Threshold frequency f0: for f < f0, no photoelectrons are emitted, regardless of light intensity.
  • For f > f0, emission of photoelectrons is almost instantaneous, even for very weak light.
  • At fixed f > f0, increasing light intensity increases the photocurrent (number of electrons emitted per second).
  • For a given metal, the maximum kinetic energy (or stopping potential Vs) depends on f, not on light intensity.

Comparison: wave model vs photon model

Experimental observation Prediction by classical wave model Explanation by photon model (Einstein)
No photoelectrons are emitted when f < f0, no matter how intense the light is. Electrons should eventually gain enough energy to escape even at low frequency if the light is intense enough or shines for a long time. Each photon has energy h f. If h f < φ, no electron can escape. Increasing intensity increases the number of photons, not the energy of each photon.
For f > f0, emission is almost instantaneous, even for very weak light. At very low intensity, electrons should need time to accumulate enough energy before leaving the metal. A single photon can transfer its energy to one electron in one interaction, so emission occurs without time lag if h f is large enough.
For a given metal, the maximum kinetic energy (or Vs) depends on f, not on intensity. Higher intensity should give more energy to each electron → larger maximum kinetic energy is expected. At fixed f, photon energy h f is fixed. Intensity affects how many photons arrive each second, not the energy per photon.
At fixed f > f0, photocurrent increases with light intensity. Higher intensity can drive more electrons away per second. Higher intensity means more photons per second striking the surface → more electrons emitted per second, so photocurrent increases.

3. Photon model of light

  • Light consists of particles called photons.
  • Energy of each photon: E = h f
  • At a given f, intensity Int is proportional to the number of photons incident per second per unit area. Higher intensity → more photons per second → larger photocurrent.
  • For a monochromatic beam of power P and frequency f, number of photons per second is Ph f.
HKDSE reminder: You must be able to relate intensity (fixed f) to the number of photons. Millikan’s photoelectric experiment is not required.

4. Intensity of a torch (photon view)

Definition: Int = PA
Photon link: energy delivered in time t is E = N h f, so
P = Et = N h ft  →  Int = PA = N h fA t
At fixed f, higher intensity means larger Nt (more photons per second per unit area), not larger energy per photon.

5. Key terms

Photoelectric effect: emission of electrons from a metal surface when electromagnetic radiation shines on it.

Photon: a particle of light. Energy of each photon is E = h f.

Photoelectron: an electron emitted from the metal surface due to the photoelectric effect.

Work function φ: minimum energy needed for an electron to escape from a metal surface.

Threshold frequency f0: the minimum frequency needed to produce photoelectrons.

Stopping potential Vs: the minimum retarding potential magnitude that just stops the most energetic photoelectrons so the photocurrent falls to zero.

Photocurrent: current in the external circuit due to the flow of photoelectrons.

Intensity Int: power per unit area, Int = PA. At fixed f, higher Int means more photons per second per unit area, not more energy per photon.

6. Common traps

  • Trap: “Higher intensity means each photon has higher energy.” Fix: photon energy depends on f only: E = h f. Intensity changes the number of photons per second.
  • Trap: “At low intensity, there must be a time delay.” Fix: emission is (almost) instantaneous when f > f0.
  • Trap: “If f < f0, increasing intensity will eventually cause emission.” Fix: no emission occurs because h f is too small.
