1.1 Basic arrangement Setup

  • Monochromatic light of frequency f shines on a metal cathode in vacuum.
  • The cathode and anode form a photocell connected to a microammeter and a variable d.c. power supply to measure the photocurrent for different applied potentials.
HKDSE note: use of gold-leaf electroscope and practical applications of photocells are not required in the syllabus; only the basic photocell arrangement and its behaviour are examined.

1.2 Main observations Evidence

  • Threshold frequency f0: for f < f0, no photoelectrons are emitted, regardless of light intensity.
  • For f > f0, emission of photoelectrons is almost instantaneous, even for very weak light.
  • At fixed f > f0, increasing light intensity increases the photocurrent (number of electrons emitted per second).
  • For a given metal, the maximum kinetic energy (or stopping potential) of the photoelectrons depends only on f, not on light intensity.

Comparison: wave model vs photon model

Experimental observation Prediction by classical wave model Explanation by photon model (Einstein)
No photoelectrons are emitted when f < f0, no matter how intense the light is. Electrons should eventually gain enough energy to escape even at low frequency if the light is intense enough or shines for a long time. Emission is expected for any frequency. Each photon has energy hf. If hf < φ, no electron can escape. Increasing intensity only increases the number of photons, not the energy of each photon.
For f > f0, emission of photoelectrons is almost instantaneous, even for very weak light. At very low intensity, the wave carries little power, so surface electrons should need a noticeable time to accumulate enough energy before leaving the metal. A single photon can give all its energy to one electron in one interaction, so emission occurs without time lag provided hf > φ.
For a given metal, the maximum kinetic energy (or stopping potential) of photoelectrons depends only on f, not on light intensity. If light is a wave, higher intensity (larger amplitude) should give more energy to each electron → larger maximum kinetic energy is expected. Einstein’s equation Kmax = hf − φ shows that the maximum energy depends only on frequency. Intensity changes the number of emitted electrons, not Kmax.
At fixed f > f0, photocurrent increases with light intensity. Higher intensity means a stronger wave, which can drive more electrons away from the metal per second. Higher intensity means more photons per second striking the surface → more electrons are emitted per second, so photocurrent increases.

1.3 Photon model of light Model

  • Light consists of particles called photons.
  • Energy of each photon: E = h f where h is Planck constant and f is frequency.
  • At a given f, the intensity I of the light is proportional to the number of photons incident per second per unit area. Higher intensity → more photons per second → more photoelectrons are emitted (larger photocurrent).
  • For a monochromatic beam of power P and frequency f, the number of photons emitted per second is P ÷ (h f).

1.4 Einstein’s photoelectric equation Formula

Energy conservation for one photon hitting one surface electron:

h f = φ + 12 me vmax2 Kmax = 12 me vmax2 = h f - φ
  • φ (phi): work function of the metal (minimum energy needed to just remove an electron from the surface).
  • vmax: maximum speed of emitted photoelectrons.
  • Electrons inside a metal have different initial energies and depths. As a result, photoelectrons are emitted with a range of kinetic energies from 0 up to Kmax.

Threshold frequency

At threshold, electrons just escape with zero kinetic energy:

φ = h f0

The corresponding threshold wavelength λ0 satisfies f0 = cλ0, so λ0 = cf0.

  • Different metals have different work functions, so they have different threshold frequencies (and threshold wavelengths).
  • A smaller work function → lower threshold frequency (larger threshold wavelength), so electrons are more easily emitted.
1 eV = 1.60 × 10−19 J

Stopping potential

When a retarding potential Vs just stops the most energetic electrons:

e Vs = 12 me vmax2 = h f - φ
Graph of Vs against f is a straight line with slope he and intercept −φe.

Alternative form in terms of wavelength

Since c = fλ, Einstein’s equation can also be written as

Kmax = h f - φ = h cλ - φ

Therefore, a graph of Kmax against 1λ is a straight line with:

  • slope h c (same for all metals)
  • vertical intercept −φ
  • horizontal intercept at 1λ0, which gives the threshold wavelength λ0

Photocurrent versus applied potential (qualitative)

  • At V = 0, some photoelectrons still reach the anode → a non-zero photocurrent flows if f > f0.
  • As the anode is made more positive (increasing accelerating potential), more emitted electrons are collected and the photocurrent increases, then reaches a saturation value when all emitted electrons are collected.
  • If the anode is made negative, the potential retards the electrons. The photocurrent decreases and becomes zero at the stopping potential Vs, when even the most energetic photoelectrons can just no longer reach the anode.
  • For a given frequency f > f0, increasing the light intensity increases the number of photons incident per second. More electrons are emitted per second, so the saturation photocurrent increases but the stopping potential Vs remains unchanged.
  • For a given intensity, increasing the frequency (above f0) increases the maximum kinetic energy Kmax of photoelectrons, so the stopping potential Vs becomes more negative in magnitude, while the saturation photocurrent stays approximately the same.

1.5 Significance Why?

  • The photoelectric effect provides strong evidence that light has a particle nature (photon model).
  • It shows that energy exchange between light and matter occurs in discrete packets of size h f.

1.6 Key terms for HKDSE (Photoelectric effect) Glossary

Photoelectric effect: emission of electrons from the surface of a metal when electromagnetic radiation of sufficiently high frequency is incident on it.

Photon: a quantum (packet) of light energy with energy E = h f, where h is Planck constant and f is the frequency of the light.

Photoelectron: an electron emitted from a metal surface as a result of the photoelectric effect.

Work function φ: the minimum energy required to just remove an electron from the surface of a metal. It can be expressed in joule (J) or electron‑volt (eV).

φ = h f0

Threshold frequency f0: the minimum frequency of incident radiation that can just cause photoelectrons to be emitted from a metal surface. For f < f0, no photoelectrons are emitted, no matter how intense the light is.

Stopping potential Vs: the smallest retarding potential applied between anode and cathode that just stops the most energetic photoelectrons so that the photocurrent falls to zero.

Photocurrent: the electric current in the external circuit due to the motion of photoelectrons emitted from the cathode and collected by the anode.

HKDSE common traps & mistakes (Photoelectric effect)

  • Mixing up the roles of frequency and intensity: Kmax and Vs depend on f only (for f > f0), while photocurrent and saturation current depend mainly on intensity.
  • Not recognising from a current–voltage graph that the photocurrent reaches a saturation value at some positive potential, and thinking it will keep increasing indefinitely.
  • Confusing maximum kinetic energy, work function and stopping potential, and treating them as if they were the same quantity.
  • Using hf = eVs directly without subtracting the work function. Correct relation: eVs = hf - φ.
  • Forgetting that no photoelectrons are emitted when f < f0 (or λ > λ0), no matter how strong the light is.
  • Not converting properly between eV and J when substituting into equations.
  • Misreading a graph of Vs against f or Kmax against 1λ: assuming the straight line must pass through the origin, confusing the slope, or not identifying the threshold frequency / threshold wavelength from the axis intercepts.
