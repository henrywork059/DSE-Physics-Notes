E2 — Nanotechnology: Applications & Hazards

HKDSE Physics — Elective E2: Atomic World · Recent developments in nanotechnology (daily-life applications and safety issues).

← Back to index

Nanotechnology develops and uses materials and devices at the nano scale (about 1–100 nm). Nano materials can show special properties, leading to many useful applications, but there are also concerns about risks and safety.

Exam focus: examples of applications + explain hazards & safety concerns Skills: link “nano” to special properties (surface area, structure) Key message: still developing; long-term effects uncertain

1. What is nanotechnology? Define

  • Nano means \(10^{-9}\). \(1\,\text{nm} = 10^{-9}\,\text{m}\).
  • Nanotechnology deals with the design, fabrication or use of materials and devices at the nano scale (typically 1–100 nm).
  • Nano materials can exist in different forms such as nanoparticles, nanotubes and nanowires.
HKDSE Scope
You are expected to describe examples of applications and discuss hazards and safety issues. Detailed quantum mechanics is not required.

2. Why materials at nano scale can be different Explain

(A) Large surface-area-to-volume ratio

  • When size decreases, the percentage of particles at the surface increases.
  • This can make nano materials more reactive and more effective as catalysts (more surface available).
Quick check (ratio idea)

Surface-area-to-volume ratio increases when size decreases.

Dividing a solid into many tiny pieces greatly increases total surface area (even if total volume stays the same).

(B) Different atomic arrangements (structure)

  • Different arrangements of atoms can lead to different physical properties.
  • In HKDSE, examples are often illustrated using different forms of carbon (e.g. nanotube vs other forms).
  • Only the following types of properties are emphasised: optical (colour, transparency), mechanical (strength, hardness) and electrical (conductivity).
Boundary
A full explanation may require quantum mechanics, which is out of scope. In exam answers, state the observed property change and link it to nano size (surface/structure).

3. Recent developments & daily-life applications Examples

Note
HKDSE expects applications in three areas: Materials, Information technology, and Health care & Environment.
Area Typical applications (HKDSE examples) Key idea (why nano helps)
Materials
  • Stain-resistant fibres
  • Anti-bacterial / detoxicating / de-odorising nano paint or coatings
  • Strong / flexible / light / conductive materials (e.g. composites containing carbon nanotubes)
 Large surface area; special structures can improve strength, reduce mass, or change conductivity.
Information technology
  • Better data storage
  • Better computation (smaller, faster components)
 Smaller structures allow higher density of components.
Health care & Environment
  • Chemical and biological sensors
  • Drugs and delivery devices
  • Clean energy; clean air and water
 High sensitivity (large surface); catalysts; special surface effects.
More concrete examples you can quote in short answers
  • TiO2 nanoparticles can act as a photocatalyst under UV, giving detoxicating and anti-bacterial effects (nano form increases surface area).
  • Silver nanoparticles can have detoxicating, deodorising and antibacterial properties (e.g. used in air/water treatment).
  • Nano-coatings can be water-repelling and self-cleaning, helping prevent ice and dirt on surfaces.
  • Lubricants with nanoparticles can reduce friction.
Important
Nanotechnology is still developing. Applications are growing, but long-term effects are still under investigation.

4. Potential hazards, risks and safety concerns Discuss

A substance harmless at ordinary size may become harmful in nano size. Some experiments suggest possible risks, but scientists are still unable to come to a definite conclusion.

(A) How nano materials may enter the human body

  • Inhalation: free nano materials may be released during manufacturing or by abrasion of products and be inhaled into the lungs.
  • Through skin: nano materials in products in direct contact with us (e.g. sunscreens, cosmetics) may penetrate skin.
  • Ingestion: nano materials in food or beverages may enter the body when eaten.

(B) Examples of possible hazards (based on experiments)

  • Some nano materials (e.g. carbon nanotubes, ZnO nanoparticles) may cause damage to the lungs when inhaled.
  • Some nano materials may enter the bloodstream and then reach other organs (e.g. brain), causing damage.
  • Some nano materials may facilitate the production of reactive molecules that may lead to cell damage.
Key exam message
The long-term effects on safety, health and the environment are still under investigation. So risk assessment and safe use are important.
Safety (reasonable measures you can mention)
  • Control release of free nanoparticles (sealed processes, filters).
  • Personal protective equipment (masks/respirators, gloves) when handling nano powders.
  • Proper labelling, regulation and disposal to reduce environmental release.
  • More testing and monitoring before wide use in products.

5. Discussion points (for long questions / SBA) Think

  • Benefits: better materials, better sensors/medical uses, cleaner air/water, improved energy.
  • Concerns: unknown long-term health and environmental effects; possible toxicity of free nanoparticles; control and regulation.
  • Balanced conclusion: nanotechnology brings many benefits, but should be developed with careful safety evaluation.
HKDSE Tip
In a “discuss” question, give both sides (advantages + potential risks) and end with a reasonable, balanced statement.

6. Common traps Avoid

  • Over-explaining with quantum mechanics: not required. State that quantum effect may be involved, but keep it brief.
  • Only listing benefits: exam questions often require hazards / safety issues as well.
  • Vague wording: give at least one concrete example (e.g. nano paint, CNT composites, nano sensors, drug delivery).
  • Mixing up TEM/STM content: this page is about applications & hazards, not detailed microscope operation.

7. Key terms Glossary

  • Nanotechnology: technology involving materials/devices at nano scale (typically 1–100 nm).
  • Nanoparticle: a particle with size in nano scale.
  • Nanotube / nanowire: nano-scale tube-like / wire-like structures.
  • Surface-area-to-volume ratio: ratio that increases greatly when size decreases, making surface effects more important.
  • Risk assessment: evaluating potential hazards and safety concerns before wide application.
