2.1 Structure of the atom (Rutherford model) Model

  • An atom consists of a tiny nucleus and electrons around it.
  • All positive charge and most of the mass of the atom are concentrated in the nucleus.
  • Electrons orbit the nucleus under the electric force of attraction.
  • The rest of the atom is largely empty space.
A scientific model is proposed to explain observations and may be modified or replaced if it fails to explain new observations.

2.2 α-particle scattering (evidence) Evidence

Key observations

  • Nearly all α particles pass straight through a thin metal foil.
  • A very small number are deflected by large angles, and a few even bounce back.

Conclusions (link observation → model)

  • Since most α particles pass through, the atom is mostly empty space.
  • Large-angle deflections and backscattering imply a small, dense, positively charged nucleus that can strongly repel α particles.

Why only a few are deflected a lot?

  • α particles passing far from nuclei feel little repulsion and continue almost straight.
  • If an α particle passes very close to a nucleus, the repulsive electric force is very strong, so the deflection is large (or it may bounce back).
  • Collisions with electrons cause negligible change in path because electrons have much smaller mass than an α particle.
Syllabus boundary: You are expected to realise the importance of scattering experiments, but the detailed setups of different scattering experiments and the names of new particles are not required.

2.3 Limitations of Rutherford’s model Why it fails

  • Atomic stability problem: an electron in circular motion is accelerating. According to classical electromagnetism, it should emit electromagnetic waves, lose energy and spiral into the nucleus. The atom would collapse, but atoms are stable.
  • Line spectra problem: in Rutherford’s model, the electron orbital frequency can take any value. It would emit electromagnetic waves with a continuous range of frequencies. However, monatomic gases produce discrete line spectra.
These limitations show that Rutherford’s model must be modified — leading to Bohr’s model with quantised orbits/energy levels.

2.4 Importance of scattering experiments Big idea

  • Scattering experiments with energetic particles are a major tool for studying the structure of atoms and nuclei.
  • They can also contribute to searching for new particles in modern physics (details and names not required).

2.5 Key terms (Rutherford topic) Glossary

Rutherford’s atomic model: an atom has a tiny nucleus containing all positive charge and most mass, with electrons around it; the rest of the atom is largely empty space.

Nucleus: the tiny central core of an atom where positive charge and most of the atom’s mass are concentrated.

α particle: a positively charged particle emitted by some radioactive sources, used as the incident particles in the scattering experiment.

Scattering: a process in which particles (or waves) are deflected from their original paths by a force field.

Limitation of a model: a situation where the model cannot correctly explain an observation (e.g. atomic stability or line spectra), so the model needs modification or replacement.

Line spectrum: an emission (or absorption) spectrum showing discrete lines at certain wavelengths/frequencies, indicating discrete energy differences in atoms.

HKDSE common traps & mistakes (Rutherford topic)

  • Stating “most α particles pass through” but not concluding that the atom is mostly empty space.
  • Saying “nucleus is positive” but not linking it to repulsion of α particles and large-angle deflection.
  • Mixing up the limitation: don’t say Rutherford model “cannot explain why atoms are neutral” (neutrality is not a difficulty for the model).
  • Explaining line spectra using the wrong idea (e.g. “electrons lose some energy and leave”). Correct point: Rutherford model predicts a continuous emission spectrum, but observed spectra are line spectra.
  • Going beyond syllabus by describing detailed experimental setups of other scattering experiments or naming newly discovered particles.
