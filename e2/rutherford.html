1. Structure of the atom (Rutherford model) Model

  • An atom consists of a tiny nucleus and electrons around it.
  • All positive charge and most of the mass of the atom are concentrated in the nucleus.
  • Electrons orbit the nucleus under the electric force of attraction.
  • The rest of the atom is largely empty space (nucleus is tiny compared with the whole atom).
A scientific model is proposed to explain observations and may be modified or replaced if it fails to explain new observations.

2. α-particle scattering (evidence) Evidence

Key observations

  • Nearly all α particles pass straight through a thin metal foil.
  • A very small number are deflected by large angles, and a few even bounce back.

Conclusions (link observation → model)

  • Since most α particles pass through, the atom is mostly empty space.
  • Large-angle deflections and backscattering imply a small, dense, positively charged nucleus that can strongly repel α particles.
Observation Conclusion (Rutherford model)
Most α particles pass straight through the foil. Atom is mostly empty space.
Very few are deflected by large angles / bounce back. Positive charge (and most mass) is concentrated in a small nucleus which can cause strong repulsion when an α particle passes close.

Experiment essentials (often tested)

  • Thin gold foil: so each α particle would most likely be scattered by one nucleus only (not multiple nuclei).
  • In vacuum: reduce collisions with air molecules so the scattering is mainly due to the foil.
  • Why not use β particles? β particles are much lighter and can be deflected noticeably by both nuclei and electrons, so the result would not give clear evidence about a small nucleus.
The backscattering result is strong evidence against the idea that positive charge is spread out over the whole atom (you do NOT need the detailed Thomson model description).

What can / cannot be concluded

  • Can conclude: nucleus is positively charged; atom is mostly empty space; nucleus contains most mass.
  • Cannot conclude: the composition of the nucleus (e.g. “protons + neutrons”) from this experiment alone.

3. Limitations of Rutherford’s model Why it fails

  • Atomic stability problem: an electron in circular motion is accelerating. In classical physics, it should emit electromagnetic waves continuously, lose energy and spiral into the nucleus. The atom would collapse, but atoms are stable.
  • Line spectra problem: if electron energy can change continuously, the emitted electromagnetic waves would have a continuous range of frequencies. However, monatomic gases produce discrete line spectra.
These limitations show that Rutherford’s model must be modified — leading to Bohr’s model with quantised energy levels.

Common 2-mark add-on: Rutherford vs Bohr

  • Similarity: both have a tiny nucleus with electrons moving around it (orbital picture).
  • Difference: Bohr’s model has quantised orbits / energy levels; Rutherford’s model does not.

4. Importance of scattering experiments Big idea

  • Scattering experiments with energetic particles are a major tool for studying the structure of atoms and nuclei.
  • They also have impact on searching for new particles in modern physics (detailed setups and names are not required).

5. Key terms (Rutherford topic) Glossary

Geiger–Marsden experiment: a thin metal foil is bombarded by α particles and the deflection pattern is observed. The results give evidence for a tiny, positively charged nucleus and that the atom is mostly empty space.

Rutherford’s atomic model: an atom has a tiny nucleus containing all positive charge and most mass, with electrons around it; the rest of the atom is largely empty space.

Nucleus: the tiny central core of an atom where positive charge and most of the atom’s mass are concentrated.

α particle: a positively charged particle emitted by some radioactive sources, used as the incident particles in the scattering experiment.

Scattering: a process in which particles (or waves) are deflected from their original paths by a force field.

Limitation of a model: a situation where the model cannot correctly explain an observation (e.g. atomic stability or line spectra), so the model needs modification or replacement.

Line spectrum: an emission (or absorption) spectrum showing discrete lines at certain wavelengths/frequencies, indicating discrete energy differences in atoms.

6. HKDSE common traps & mistakes Traps

  • Stating “most α particles pass through” but not concluding that the atom is mostly empty space.
  • Saying “nucleus is positive” but not linking it to repulsion of α particles and large-angle deflection.
  • Mixing up the limitation: don’t say Rutherford model “cannot explain why atoms are neutral”.
  • For the experiment details: don’t claim “gold foil was used because gold contains free electrons” (wrong reason).
  • Over-claiming: Geiger–Marsden results do not tell you the nucleus is made of protons and neutrons.
  • Going beyond syllabus by describing detailed setups of other scattering experiments or naming newly discovered particles.
