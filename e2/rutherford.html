2.1 Structure of the atom (Rutherford model) Model

  • An atom consists of a tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus at the centre.
  • Almost all the mass of the atom is concentrated in the nucleus.
  • Electrons move around the nucleus.
  • Most of the volume of an atom is empty space between the nucleus and the electrons.
HKDSE note: You only need to describe the structure. Numerical estimates of nuclear radius or detailed nuclear composition are not required here.

2.2 α-particle scattering (gold-foil experiment) Evidence

Basic idea

  • A narrow beam of α-particles is directed at a very thin metal foil (e.g. gold).
  • A fluorescent screen around the foil detects where the α-particles arrive.
HKDSE note: Detailed design or names of detectors are not required; focus on the observations and conclusions.

Main observations

  • Most α-particles pass straight through the foil or are only slightly deflected.
  • A small fraction of α-particles are deflected through large angles.
  • A very few α-particles are scattered backwards (rebound).

Conclusions from scattering

  • Since most α-particles pass straight through, atoms must be mostly empty space.
  • Large-angle deflections and backscattering show that there is a small, dense, positively charged nucleus which strongly repels α-particles when they come very close.
  • Because only a tiny fraction of α-particles suffer large deflection, the nucleus must occupy only a very small volume compared with the whole atom.

Comparison with Thomson's "plum pudding" model

  • In Thomson's model, positive charge and mass are spread uniformly throughout the atom.
  • If charge and mass were spread out, α-particles would not be deflected through very large angles.
  • The observed large-angle scattering therefore contradicts Thomson's model and supports Rutherford's nuclear model.

2.3 Limitations of Rutherford's model Problems

  • According to classical electromagnetism, an electron moving in a circular orbit is an accelerating charge and should radiate energy continuously.
  • If electrons radiate energy, they would lose energy and spiral into the nucleus in a very short time → the atom would be unstable.
  • Real atoms are stable, so Rutherford's model cannot explain atomic stability.
  • Rutherford's model also cannot explain discrete line spectra of atoms (it would predict continuous spectra instead).
Link to Bohr: Bohr's model introduces quantised energy levels and stationary orbits to explain both stability and line spectra.

2.4 Importance of scattering experiments Significance

  • Rutherford's α-particle scattering experiment provided direct evidence for the nuclear model of the atom.
  • Scattering experiments are powerful tools to probe the internal structure of atoms and nuclei.
  • Similar ideas are used in modern physics to investigate smaller particles and to search for new particles (details not required in HKDSE).

2.5 Key terms for HKDSE (Rutherford’s atomic model) Glossary

Rutherford’s atomic model: an atomic model in which an atom consists of a tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus containing almost all the mass, with electrons moving around it and most of the volume being empty space.

Nucleus: the small, dense, positively charged core at the centre of an atom containing almost all of the atom’s mass.

α-particle: a positively charged particle (helium nucleus) with charge +2e and relatively large mass, often used as a probe in scattering experiments.

Scattering experiment: an experiment in which a beam of particles (e.g. α-particles) is directed at a target and the directions of the scattered particles are observed to deduce the internal structure of the target.

Thomson’s “plum pudding” model: an earlier atomic model in which positive charge and mass were spread more or less uniformly throughout the atom with electrons embedded inside, which cannot explain the observed large-angle α-particle scattering.

Line spectrum: a spectrum that consists of discrete bright or dark lines at particular wavelengths, showing that the atom can have only certain allowed energies (detailed treatment in the Bohr’s model section).

HKDSE tip: Be able to match each observation in the α-particle scattering experiment with a correct conclusion about atomic structure, and use these key terms precisely in explanation questions.

HKDSE common traps & mistakes (Rutherford's model)

  • Saying that most α-particles are deflected; in fact, most pass straight through, showing atoms are mostly empty space.
  • Describing the nucleus as large or occupying most of the volume; it should be described as tiny but containing almost all the mass and positive charge.
  • Explaining the instability of Rutherford's model only by “electrons are attracted to the nucleus” without mentioning that an accelerating charge radiates energy.
  • Giving detailed apparatus designs or names of modern experiments and particles, which are not required in HKDSE.
  • Failing to link each observation (e.g. most pass through, some deflect) to a clear conclusion in explanation questions.
