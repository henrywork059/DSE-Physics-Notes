Nano Scale & Optical Resolution (Rayleigh criterion)

Why optical microscopes cannot see nano-sized details (diffraction limit).

1. Big idea: why optical microscopes have a limit

In HKDSE, the key limitation discussed for optical microscopes is diffraction of light at the aperture. (Spherical / chromatic aberrations are not required.)

Resolution (or resolving power) is the ability to distinguish two nearby points as two separate points.

When light from two points passes through a finite aperture (e.g. pupil / objective lens), each point forms a diffraction pattern. If the patterns overlap too much, the points cannot be distinguished.

2. Key terms (HKDSE wording)

Resolved / not resolved

  • Resolved: two points can be distinguished as two separate points.
  • Not resolved: the images overlap so much that they appear as one.

Angles & aperture

  • Angular separation: angle between lines of sight to two points.
  • Angular resolution θR: minimum angular separation that can be resolved (in rad).
  • Aperture: the opening of the instrument (diameter d).
  • λ: wavelength of light used.

3. Rayleigh criterion (concept)

Two points can just be resolved when:

The central maximum of the diffraction pattern of one point is centred on the first-order minimum of the diffraction pattern of the other.

(The detailed reason for bright/dark fringes is beyond this topic.)

4. Formula & trends

Rayleigh criterion (angular resolution)
θR ≈ 1.22 λ / d
(Derivation is not required.)

How to increase resolving power

  • Use shorter wavelength (smaller λ)
  • Use a larger aperture (larger d)

Because smaller θR means higher resolving power.

Common MCQ trap

  • Increasing brightness does not increase resolving power.
  • Moving closer to an object increases its angular separation, so it may become resolvable.

5. Minimum resolvable distance on the object

If the object (or two points on a sample) is at distance r from the aperture, then for small angles:

Minimum resolvable distance
s ≈ r θR
  • s: shortest distance between two points that can be resolved
  • r: distance from aperture to object (e.g. objective lens to sample)

6. Worked example (optical microscope)

Suppose an optical microscope has:

  • aperture diameter d = 6 mm = 6 × 10⁻³ m
  • distance to sample r = 1 cm = 0.01 m
  • wavelength λ = 500 nm = 5.00 × 10⁻⁷ m
Calculation
θR ≈ 1.22λ/d = 1.22(5.00×10⁻⁷)/(6×10⁻³)
s ≈ rθR = 0.01 × 1.22(5.00×10⁻⁷)/(6×10⁻³)
= 1.02 × 10⁻⁶ m
Conclusion: the minimum resolvable detail is about 1 μm, so a school optical microscope is not good enough to observe nano materials (1–100 nm).
Mini-check: red vs violet (ratio thinking)
For the same aperture d, θR ∝ λ. Red light has a longer wavelength (~700 nm) than violet (~400 nm), so red gives larger θR → lower resolving power.

7. DSE checkpoints (fast marks)

Checklist answers (short & exam-style)

  • What limits resolving power of an optical microscope? Diffraction of light at the aperture (Rayleigh criterion).
  • How to increase resolving power? Use shorter wavelength / larger aperture.
  • If observer walks towards two lamps: angular separation increases → easier to resolve.
  • If lamps become brighter: resolving power unchanged (brightness does not change θR).
Quick MCQ pattern: compare two instruments
If two instruments use the same λ, then θR ∝ 1/d. Larger d → smaller θR → higher resolving power.
