6.1 Big idea: why optical microscopes have a limit Concept

  • The key limitation (HKDSE) is diffraction of light at a finite aperture (e.g. objective lens / pupil).
  • Each point image is not a perfect point: it spreads into a diffraction pattern.
  • If two diffraction patterns overlap too much, two points appear as one.
Reminder: Aberrations (spherical/chromatic) are not the focus here. For this note, use diffraction + Rayleigh criterion.

6.2 Key terms (HKDSE wording) Terms

Resolved / not resolved
  • Resolved: two points can be distinguished as two separate points.
  • Not resolved: images overlap so much that they appear as one.
Angular resolution
  • Angular separation: angle between lines of sight to the two points.
  • Angular resolution θR: minimum angular separation that can be resolved (rad).
  • Aperture diameter d and wavelength λ affect θR.

6.3 Rayleigh criterion Idea

Two point images are just resolved when:

The central maximum of one diffraction pattern is centred on the first minimum of the other.
The detailed formation of maxima/minima is not required. Use the criterion as a stated condition.

6.4 Formula & trends Formula

Rayleigh criterion (angular resolution)

θR ≈ 1.22 λ / d
Derivation is not required.

How to increase resolving power

  • Use shorter wavelength (smaller λ)
  • Use a larger aperture (larger d)
Exam sentence: Smaller θR means higher resolving power.
Common MCQ pattern: brightness vs resolution
Increasing brightness does not change resolving power. Resolution depends on λ and d (diffraction), not intensity.

6.5 Minimum resolvable distance on the object Apply

For small angles, when the object is at distance r from the aperture:

s ≈ r θR
  • s: minimum separation of two points on the object that can be resolved (m)
  • r: distance from aperture to object (m)
Combine the two formulas to get the trend:
s ≈ r(1.22λ/d)

6.6 Worked example (optical microscope) Example

Suppose an optical microscope has:

  • aperture diameter d = 6 mm = 6 × 10−3 m
  • distance to sample r = 1 cm = 1.0 × 10−2 m
  • wavelength λ = 500 nm = 5.00 × 10−7 m
Step 1: θR ≈ 1.22λ/d
θR ≈ 1.22(5.00×10−7)/(6×10−3) = 1.02×10−4 rad
Step 2: s ≈ rθR
s ≈ (1.0×10−2)(1.02×10−4) = 1.02×10−6 m
Conclusion: minimum resolvable detail is about 1 μm. So an optical microscope is not suitable for nano materials (1–100 nm).
Mini-check: red vs violet (ratio thinking)
For the same d, θR ∝ λ. Red light has longer wavelength than violet, so red gives larger θR → lower resolving power.

Common traps (fast marks) Traps

  • Brightness trap: intensity does not change resolving power; diffraction depends on λ and d.
  • Approaching the object: angular separation increases, so it may become resolvable (even if θR is unchanged).
  • Units: keep everything in metres and radians; convert nm and mm carefully.
  • Nano-scale meaning: 1–100 nm is much smaller than typical optical resolution (~1 μm).
