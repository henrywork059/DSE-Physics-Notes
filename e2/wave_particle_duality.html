5.1 What is wave–particle duality? Concept

In classical physics, waves and particles are described differently. Experiments in the microscopic world show that:

  • Light (often described as a wave) can show particle behaviour.
  • Electrons (often described as particles) can show wave behaviour.
HKDSE focus: You should be able to state the evidences and use the de Broglie formula to link the wave and particle properties.

5.2 Evidences of duality Evidence

(A) Light

  • Wave nature: interference and diffraction patterns.
  • Particle nature: photoelectric effect (photons eject electrons from a metal surface).

(B) Electrons (matter)

  • Particle behaviour: electrons have mass and charge; they carry momentum and can be detected as individual impacts.
  • Wave behaviour: electrons can form diffraction and interference patterns.
Typical observations (electron diffraction):
  • Electron beam onto a thin metal foil / crystal → a diffraction pattern of bright and dark rings.
  • Electron beam through a very small double slit → an interference pattern.
These give strong support to the wave nature of electrons.

5.3 de Broglie wavelength Formula

de Broglie proposed that a moving particle is associated with a wavelength (a “matter wave”):

λ = hp
  • λ: de Broglie wavelength (m)
  • h: Planck constant (J s)
  • p: momentum (kg m s−1)

For a non-relativistic particle, p = mv, so

λ = hmv
Important trend: larger momentum p → smaller wavelength λ.

5.4 Problem skills (HKDSE style) Apply

(A) Compare wavelengths (same speed)

If two particles travel at the same speed, then λ ∝ 1/m.

  • If mass doubles → wavelength halves.
  • If mass is half → wavelength doubles.

(B) Given kinetic energy K

Using K = p2 / (2m) (non-relativistic),

p = (2mK)1/2  →  λ = h(2mK)1/2

(C) Numerical examples (order of magnitude)

Example 1: 50 g ball at 60 m s−1

Formula: λ = h / (mv)

Substitute: λ = 6.63×10−34 / (0.050 × 60)

Answer: λ ≈ 2.21×10−34 m (extremely small, no observable diffraction).

Example 2: Electron at 5.0×106 m s−1

Formula: λ = h / (mv)

Answer: λ ≈ 1.46×10−10 m (comparable to atomic spacing, diffraction possible).

Example 3: Electron of kinetic energy 10 keV

  • Convert: 10 keV = 10×103 eV = 1.60×10−15 J
  • Use p = (2mK)1/2, then λ = h/p
  • Answer: λ ≈ 1.23×10−11 m
Link to microscopes: If an electron is accelerated through a p.d. V, then its kinetic energy is approximately K = eV (in J). So you may use
λ = h(2me eV)1/2
to estimate the voltage needed for λ of the order of atomic size.

5.5 Key terms Terms

  • Wave–particle duality: both wave and particle properties can be shown by the same object, depending on the experiment.
  • de Broglie wavelength: wavelength associated with a moving particle, given by λ = h/p.
  • Diffraction condition (idea): diffraction is significant when the wavelength is comparable to the spacing/size involved (atomic spacing is typically about 10−10 m).

Common traps

  • Trend: larger momentum (bigger p) → smaller de Broglie wavelength (smaller λ).
  • Unit conversion: 1 eV = 1.60×10−19 J (needed when kinetic energy is in eV or keV).
  • Over-adding extras: relativistic corrections are not required for standard HKDSE questions in this topic. Use the non-relativistic relations above unless the question explicitly provides otherwise.
