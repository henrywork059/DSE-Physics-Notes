5.1 What is wave–particle duality? Concept

In classical physics, waves and particles are very different. In the microscopic world, experiments show that:

  • Light (well known as a wave) can show particle behaviour.
  • Electrons (well known as particles) can show wave behaviour.
HKDSE focus: You should be able to describe the evidences and use the de Broglie formula to link the wave and particle properties.

5.2 Evidences of duality Evidence

(A) Light

  • Wave nature: interference and diffraction patterns.
  • Particle nature: photoelectric effect (photons eject electrons from a metal surface).

(B) Electrons (matter)

  • Particle behaviour: electrons have mass and charge; they carry momentum and can be counted as individual impacts.
  • Wave behaviour: electrons can form diffraction and interference patterns.
Typical experimental observations:
  • Electron beam onto a thin metal foil / crystal → a diffraction pattern of bright and dark rings.
  • Electron beam through a double slit (very small slit width) → an interference pattern.
These give strong support to the wave nature of electrons.

5.3 de Broglie wavelength Formula

de Broglie proposed that a moving particle is associated with a wavelength (“matter wave”):

λ = \(\dfrac{h}{p}\)
  • λ: de Broglie wavelength (m)
  • h: Planck constant (J s)
  • p: momentum (kg m s–1)

For a non-relativistic particle, p = mv, so λ = h/(mv).

Important trend: larger momentum p → smaller wavelength λ.

5.4 Problem skills (HKDSE style) Apply

(A) Compare wavelengths (same speed)

If two particles travel at the same speed, then λ \(\propto\) 1/m.

  • If mass doubles → wavelength halves.
  • If mass is half → wavelength doubles.

(B) Given kinetic energy K

Using K = p2/(2m) (non-relativistic),

p = \(\sqrt{2mK}\)   →   λ = \(\dfrac{h}{\sqrt{2mK}}\)

(C) Typical numerical examples

Example 1 (order of magnitude):

  • 50 g ball at 60 m s–1: λ = h/(mv) ≈ 2.21 × 10–34 m (extremely small, no observable diffraction).
  • Electron at 5.0 × 106 m s–1: λ ≈ 1.46 × 10–10 m (comparable to atomic spacing, diffraction possible).

This explains why wave behaviour is obvious for microscopic particles but not for everyday objects.

Example 2 (fast electron): Find λ for an electron of kinetic energy 10 keV.

  • Convert: 10 keV = 10 × 103 eV = 10 × 103 × 1.60 × 10–19 J = 1.60 × 10–15 J
  • p = \(\sqrt{2m_e K}\)
  • λ = h/p ≈ 1.23 × 10–11 m
Link to TEM (next topics): If an electron is accelerated through a p.d. V, then its kinetic energy is approximately K = eV (in J). So you may use
λ = \(\dfrac{h}{\sqrt{2m_e eV}}\)
to estimate what voltage is needed to achieve wavelengths of the order of atomic size.

5.5 Key terms Terms

Wave–particle duality

Both wave and particle properties can be shown by the same object (light or electrons), depending on the experiment.

de Broglie wavelength

The wavelength associated with a moving particle: λ = h/p.

Why diffraction happens for electrons

Diffraction is significant when the wavelength is comparable to the spacing/size involved (atomic spacing is typically about 10–10 m).

Common traps

1) Mixing up the trend

  • More momentum (bigger p) → smaller de Broglie wavelength (λ).

2) Forgetting unit conversion

  • 1 eV = 1.60 × 10–19 J (needed when K is in eV or keV).

3) Using out-of-syllabus extras

  • Relativistic corrections are not required for standard HKDSE questions in this topic. Use the non-relativistic relations given above unless the question explicitly provides otherwise.
