5.1 What is wave–particle duality? Concept

In classical physics, waves and particles are described differently. Experiments in the microscopic world show that:

  • Light (often described as a wave) can show particle behaviour.
  • Electrons (often described as particles) can show wave behaviour.
HKDSE Focus
You should be able to state the evidences and use the de Broglie formula to link the wave and particle properties.

5.2 Evidences of duality Evidence

(A) Light

  • Wave nature: interference and diffraction patterns.
  • Particle nature: photoelectric effect (photons eject electrons from a metal surface).

(B) Electrons (matter)

  • Particle behaviour: electrons have mass and charge; they carry momentum and can be detected as individual impacts.
  • Wave behaviour: electrons can form diffraction and interference patterns.
Typical Observations
  • Electron beam onto a thin metal foil / crystal → a diffraction pattern of bright and dark rings.
  • Electron beam through a very small double slit → an interference pattern.

5.3 de Broglie wavelength Formula

de Broglie proposed that a moving particle is associated with a wavelength (a “matter wave”):

\(\lambda = \frac{h}{p}\)
  • \(\lambda\): de Broglie wavelength (m)
  • \(h\): Planck constant (J s)
  • \(p\): momentum (kg m s−1)

For a non-relativistic particle, \(p = mv\), so:

\(\lambda = \frac{h}{mv}\)
Trend
Larger momentum \(p\) → smaller wavelength \(\lambda\).

5.4 Problem skills (HKDSE style) Apply

(A) Compare wavelengths (same speed)

If two particles travel at the same speed, then \(\lambda \propto 1/m\).

  • If mass doubles → wavelength halves.
  • If mass is half → wavelength doubles.

(B) Given kinetic energy \(K\)

Using \(K = p^2 / 2m\) (non-relativistic), we get \(p = \sqrt{2mK}\). Thus:

\(\lambda = \frac{h}{\sqrt{2mK}}\)

5.5 Key terms Glossary

Wave–particle duality: both wave and particle properties can be shown by the same object (e.g. light, electrons), depending on the experiment performed.

de Broglie wavelength: wavelength associated with a moving particle, given by \(\lambda = h/p\).

Diffraction condition: diffraction is significant when the wavelength is comparable to the spacing or size of the obstacle/slit (atomic spacing \(\approx 10^{-10}\,\text{m}\)).

Planck constant (h): \(h \approx 6.63 \times 10^{-34}\,\text{J s}\). Fundamental constant linking energy and frequency (\(E=hf\)) or wavelength and momentum (\(\lambda = h/p\)).

Common traps

Watch out for
  • Trend: larger momentum (bigger \(p\)) → smaller de Broglie wavelength (smaller \(\lambda\)).
  • Unit conversion: \(1\,\text{eV} = 1.60 \times 10^{-19}\,\text{J}\) (needed when kinetic energy is in eV or keV).
  • Over-adding extras: relativistic corrections are not required for standard HKDSE questions in this topic. Use non-relativistic formulas.
