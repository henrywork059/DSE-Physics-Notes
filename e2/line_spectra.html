3.1 Special features of line spectra Evidence

  • Hydrogen and other monatomic gases produce a line spectrum: discrete lines at certain wavelengths/frequencies.
  • Each line corresponds to a photon of a specific energy.
  • Therefore, line spectra are evidence that atomic energies (energy levels) are quantized.
  • The set of spectral-line frequencies is characteristic of the element (same element → same lines).
Syllabus note: The names of spectral series (e.g. Lyman, Balmer, Paschen) are not required. Focus on initial/final energy levels and the wavelength formula.

3.2 Emission spectrum and absorption spectrum Compare

Emission spectrum (bright lines)

  • An electron drops from higher level Ea to lower level Eb and emits a photon.
  • Photon energy: hf = Ea − Eb (so only discrete values are possible).
hf = hc/λ = Ea − Eb

Absorption spectrum (dark lines)

  • Light of continuous frequencies is sent through a gas.
  • Some photons with the right energy are absorbed to raise an electron from Eb to Ea.
  • Those frequencies are missing from the transmitted light, producing dark lines in a continuous spectrum.
In absorption, the absorbed photon may be re-emitted in random directions, so the forward beam shows dark lines.

3.3 Derive the wavelength formula for a transition Required

In HKDSE, you should be able to derive the expression for 1/λ using Bohr’s energy equation and E = hf.
Show the key derivation steps
  1. Photon energy for a transition:
    hf = hc/λ = Ea − Eb
  2. Bohr energy levels for hydrogen:
    En = −13.6 eV / n2
  3. Substitute Ea = −13.6/a2 eV and Eb = −13.6/b2 eV:
    hc/λ = 13.6 eV ( 1/b2 − 1/a2 )
  4. Rearrange:
    1/λa→b = (13.6 eV)/(hc) · ( 1/b2 − 1/a2 )
How to use it: Use a > b for emission (drop from higher to lower), and the expression gives a positive value of 1/λ.
The constant (13.6 eV)/(hc) is often written as R (Rydberg constant): R ≈ 1.097 × 107 m−1.

3.4 Transitions to n = 2 (four visible lines) & convergence Interpret

Balmer-type formula for final level b = 2

1/λ = R ( 1/22 − 1/n2 )    (n = 3, 4, 5, …)
  • Visible light is roughly 400–700 nm.
  • For hydrogen emission lines with final level n = 2, some transitions give wavelengths in the visible range (four visible lines).
  • As n increases, lines get closer together and approach a shortest wavelength (the convergence limit).
  • For final level n = 2, the shortest wavelength corresponds to a = ∞ (i.e. transition from n = ∞ to n = 2).
Transition (a → b) Wavelength (approx.) Region
3 → 2 656 nm Visible
4 → 2 486 nm Visible
5 → 2 434 nm Visible
6 → 2 410 nm Visible (near violet)
∞ → 2 (limit) ≈ 365 nm Ultraviolet (just below visible)
You do not need to memorise series names. In exam questions, focus on identifying the initial and final energy levels.

3.5 Worked examples (HKDSE style) Practice

Example 1: Find the wavelength for the transition 5 → 2

  • Formula: 1/λ = R (1/22 − 1/52)
  • Substitute: 1/λ = R (1/4 − 1/25) = R (21/100)
  • Result: λ = 100/(21R) ≈ 4.34 × 10−7 m = 434 nm (visible)

Example 2: Shortest wavelength for lines produced by transitions to n = 2

  • Shortest λ means largest 1/λ.
  • For final level b = 2, the largest 1/λ occurs at a = ∞.
  • Formula: 1/λmin = R (1/4 − 0) = R/4
  • Result: λmin = 4/R ≈ 3.65 × 10−7 m = 365 nm (UV)

Example 3: Can EM radiation of wavelength 360 nm be absorbed by hydrogen atoms in the first excited state (n = 2)?

  • Minimum energy to ionize from n = 2: Emin = 0 − E2 = 3.4 eV
  • Photon energy: E = hc/λ (for λ = 360 nm, E ≈ 3.45 eV)
  • Since E > 3.4 eV, the radiation can be absorbed and the atom becomes ionized.
This is a common exam point: even if the photon energy does not match any excitation energy, it can still be absorbed if it is high enough to ionize the atom.

3.6 Common traps & marking-scheme tips Avoid

  • Line spacing: In a series to n = 2, the line that is furthest away corresponds to the smallest initial n (e.g. 3 → 2), not the largest n.
  • Shortest wavelength: For transitions to n = 2, the shortest λ is from ∞ → 2 (the convergence limit), not from 6 → 2 or other finite n.
  • Ionization vs excitation: Excitation needs an exact photon energy, but ionization can happen for any photon energy above the threshold.
  • State your method: Always state the formula used (e.g. 1/λ = R(1/b2 − 1/a2)) and show substitution of a and b.

3.7 Key terms Glossary

Line spectrum: a spectrum consisting of discrete lines at certain wavelengths/frequencies, produced by (or absorbed by) atoms with discrete energy levels.

Emission spectrum: discrete bright lines produced when electrons drop to lower energy levels and emit photons of certain energies.

Absorption spectrum: dark lines in a continuous spectrum, produced when a gas absorbs photons of certain energies to raise electrons to higher energy levels.

Electron transition: an electron jumps from one energy level to another, accompanied by emission or absorption of a photon (or by energy transfer in a collision).

Wavelength formula (hydrogen): when an electron changes from level a to b,

1/λa→b = (13.6 eV)/(hc) · ( 1/b2 − 1/a2 )

R (Rydberg constant): the constant that appears in 1/λ formulas for hydrogen spectral lines. In HKDSE problems you can take R ≈ 1.097 × 107 m−1.
