E203 · probing into nano scale & microscopes

1. What does “nano-scale” mean?

  • 1 nanometre (nm) = 10−9 m.
  • Typical atom diameter: about 0.1 nm (order of magnitude).
  • At nano-scale, surface effects become important because surface area/volume ratio is large.
  • We need special microscopes because visible light wavelength (~400–700 nm) is far larger than nano-scale objects.
HKDSE key idea: Magnification is not the same as resolution. You can enlarge a blurred image, but you cannot see finer detail unless resolution improves.

2. Resolution & diffraction limit (optical microscopes)

Resolution = ability to distinguish two close objects as separate.

Resolution limit (Rayleigh/Abbe idea): d ≈ 0.61 λNA
  • d = minimum resolvable separation (smaller d → better resolution).
  • λ = wavelength of light used (shorter λ → better resolution).
  • NA = numerical aperture (larger NA → better resolution).
Numerical aperture: NA = n sinθ
  • n = refractive index of medium (air ~1, oil > 1).
  • θ = half-angle of the cone of light entering the lens.
Improving optical microscope resolution: use shorter wavelength (violet/UV), increase NA (wider lens, immersion oil).

3. Electron microscopes (TEM & SEM)

  • Electrons behave as waves (de Broglie), with wavelength much smaller than visible light.
  • Smaller wavelength → smaller diffraction spreading → much higher resolution.
de Broglie relation: pλ = h → λ = hp
  • TEM (Transmission Electron Microscope): electrons pass through thin specimen → internal structure.
  • SEM (Scanning Electron Microscope): electrons scan the surface → detailed 3D-like surface image.
Limitations (HKDSE style): requires vacuum; specimens must be prepared carefully; cannot observe living organisms directly.

4. Scanning probe microscopes (STM & AFM)

  • They build images by scanning a very sharp tip over the surface point-by-point.
  • Can achieve near atomic resolution.

STM — Scanning Tunnelling Microscope

  • Works for conducting surfaces.
  • Based on quantum tunnelling: electrons tunnel across the tiny gap tip–surface.
  • Tunnelling current changes strongly with distance → used to map surface atoms.

AFM — Atomic Force Microscope

  • Works for conductors and insulators.
  • Tip on a cantilever experiences tiny forces (attraction/repulsion).
  • Cantilever deflection is detected (often by laser reflection) → surface profile.

5. Key terms (HKDSE wording)

Resolution
The ability of an imaging system to distinguish two close objects as separate. Higher resolution means smaller minimum resolvable separation.

HKDSE common traps & mistakes (E203)

  • Mixing up magnification and resolution.
  • Saying “use higher magnification” to see nano-scale detail — correct answer should be use shorter wavelength / better resolution.
  • Forgetting diffraction: optical microscopes cannot resolve features much smaller than ~λ.
  • STM works only on conductors, but AFM can work on insulators.
  • Stating electron microscopes can image living organisms — usually false because they require vacuum.
