6.1 Why optical microscopes cannot probe nano scale Link

  • Resolution of an optical instrument is limited by diffraction (Rayleigh criterion).
  • To see smaller details, we need a much smaller wavelength than visible light.
  • Electrons can behave like waves, and their de Broglie wavelength can be extremely small when accelerated through a large potential difference.

6.2 Transmission Electron Microscope (TEM) Principle

Main idea

  • A beam of high-speed electrons passes through a very thin specimen.
  • Different parts of the specimen transmit / scatter electrons differently, forming contrast.
  • The transmitted electrons are used to form a magnified image on a screen / detector.

Why it can achieve high resolution

  • Electrons have a very small de Broglie wavelength (much smaller than visible light).
  • Smaller wavelength → smaller diffraction effect → better resolving power.

Analogy with an optical microscope (HKDSE wording)

  • Light source → electron gun (produces an electron beam).
  • Glass lenses → electromagnetic lenses (focus and form images of the electron beam).
  • Objective lens forms an image; projector lens magnifies further (similar idea to optical microscope).
  • The electron beam path must be in a vacuum to reduce collisions with air molecules.
Not required: detailed focusing mechanism of electromagnetic lenses and optical aberrations.

6.3 TEM: electron wavelength and accelerating potential Formula

Electron behaves like a wave:

de Broglie: λ = h / p

For a non-relativistic electron accelerated through a potential difference V:

  • Kinetic energy gained: K = eV (in joule)
  • Momentum: p = √(2meK) = √(2meeV)
λ = h / √(2meeV)
Key trend: larger accelerating voltage V → larger momentum → smaller wavelength λ.

6.4 Estimate the anode voltage in a TEM Estimate

HKDSE questions often ask you to estimate the accelerating voltage needed to make the electron wavelength of the order of 10−12 m (picometre) so that it is much smaller than visible light.

Worked estimate (exam style)
  • Given (typical): target λ = 4.0 × 10−12 m
  • Use: λ = h / √(2meeV)
  • Rearrange: V = h2 / (2meeλ2)
  • Substitute: h = 6.63 × 10−34 J s, me = 9.11 × 10−31 kg, e = 1.60 × 10−19 C
  • Result: V ≈ 9.4 × 104 V ≈ 1 × 105 V
Conclusion: An accelerating voltage of order 105 V gives electrons with wavelength of order 10−12 m, helping TEM achieve very high resolution.
Exam note: Relativistic correction is not required for standard HKDSE problems here. Use the non-relativistic formula unless the question states otherwise.

6.5 Scanning Tunnelling Microscope (STM) Surface

Main idea

  • A very sharp conducting tip is brought extremely close to a conducting sample surface.
  • A small potential difference is applied between the tip and the sample.
  • Electrons can cross the tiny gap, producing a tunnelling current.
  • The tunnelling current depends very strongly on the tip–sample separation, so it can be used to map the surface at atomic scale.

How an image is obtained

  • The tip scans across the surface (point by point).
  • A feedback system keeps the current constant by adjusting the tip height (constant-current mode).
  • The recorded height vs position produces a 3D surface image.
HKDSE boundary: You only need the working principle (tunnelling current depends on distance). Detailed quantum theory of tunnelling is not required.

6.6 TEM vs STM (quick comparison) Compare

TEM

  • Uses transmitted electrons through a thin specimen.
  • Can reveal internal structure (inside the sample).
  • Needs vacuum; specimen must be very thin.

STM

  • Uses tunnelling current between tip and surface.
  • Maps surface topography.
  • Requires a conducting surface (or a surface that allows tunnelling current).

Common traps (high-frequency mistakes) Avoid

  • Wrong trend: larger V → smaller λ (not larger λ).
  • Unit mistakes: if using eV/keV, convert to J when substituting into p = √(2mK).
  • TEM vs optical microscope: TEM uses electrons and electromagnetic lenses, not light and glass lenses.
  • STM image: STM does not rely on transmitted electrons; it uses tunnelling current to map the surface.
  • Going beyond syllabus: do not describe detailed lens design, aberrations, or detailed quantum derivations.
