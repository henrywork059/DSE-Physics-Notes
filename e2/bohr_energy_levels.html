1. What Bohr’s model fixed

  • Rutherford model problem: orbiting electrons should radiate continuously and spiral into the nucleus → cannot explain stable atoms and discrete line spectra.
  • Bohr’s idea: electrons can occupy only discrete orbits (labelled by \( n = 1, 2, 3, \dots \)). Each orbit corresponds to a discrete total energy \( E_n \).
HKDSE Wording
“Energy levels of the hydrogen atom” and “energy levels of the electron” are used interchangeably.

2. Bohr postulates

Classical aspect

  • Electrons are treated as particles moving in circular orbits around the nucleus.
  • The centripetal force is provided by the electric (Coulomb) force of attraction between the electron and the nucleus.

Quantum aspects

  • Only certain discrete orbits are allowed (labelled by \( n = 1, 2, 3, \dots \)). These are called stationary orbits.
  • An electron in a stationary orbit does not radiate electromagnetic energy.
  • Radiation is emitted or absorbed only when the electron jumps between energy levels.
$$ hf = \frac{hc}{\lambda} = E_a - E_b $$
Mechanism
Meaning: photon energy equals the difference between a higher and a lower energy level.
Emission: higher → lower.
Absorption (excitation): lower → higher (photon energy must match the level difference exactly).

Quantization condition (Bohr)

$$ m_e v r = \frac{nh}{2\pi} \quad (n = 1, 2, 3, \dots) $$

Or: \( 2\pi m_e v r = nh \)

HKDSE Note
Basic knowledge: angular momentum \( = m v r \). Derivation is not required.

3. Energy levels of the hydrogen atom

Bohr energy equation (hydrogen)

$$ E_n = -\frac{13.6\,\text{eV}}{n^2} \quad (n = 1, 2, 3, \dots) $$
  • The negative sign means the electron is bound to the nucleus; energy must be supplied to remove it to infinity.
  • At \( n = \infty \), the electron is free and \( E_{\infty} = 0 \).
  • Energy levels get closer together as \( n \) increases.

4. Excitation energy vs ionization energy

Excitation (exact energy needed)

  • Excitation from level \( b \) to \( a \) requires the exact energy difference:
$$ \Delta E = E_a - E_b $$
  • A photon can excite the atom only if its energy equals \( \Delta E \) exactly.

Ionization (minimum energy, not exact)

  • Ionization means the electron escapes completely to \( n = \infty \) where \( E_{\infty} = 0 \).
  • Minimum energy to ionize an atom in state \( n \):
$$ E_{\min} = E_{\infty} - E_n = 0 - E_n $$
  • The atom can absorb any photon with energy ≥ \( E_{\min} \). Any extra energy becomes kinetic energy after ionization.

Quick checkpoints

  • From \( n = 1 \) to \( n = 2 \): \( \Delta E = (-3.40) - (-13.6) = 10.2\,\text{eV} \).
  • Ionization energy from ground state: \( E_{\min} = 0 - (-13.6) = 13.6\,\text{eV} \).
  • Ionization energy from \( n = 2 \): \( E_{\min} = 0 - (-3.40) = 3.40\,\text{eV} \).

5. Worked examples

Example 1: Find the energy level at \( n = 3 \)

Formula: \( E_n = -\frac{13.6}{n^2}\,\text{eV} \)

Substitute: \( E_3 = -13.6 / 3^2 = -13.6 / 9 = -1.51\,\text{eV} \)

Answer: \( E_3 = -1.51\,\text{eV} \)

Example 2: Can a 12.2 eV photon excite a hydrogen atom in the ground state to \( n = 3 \)?

Excitation requires exact energy: \( \Delta E = E_3 - E_1 \)

Using levels: \( \Delta E = (-1.51) - (-13.6) = 12.09\,\text{eV} \)

Compare: \( 12.2\,\text{eV} \neq 12.09\,\text{eV} \)

Conclusion: No, the photon cannot excite the atom (it will not be absorbed for excitation).

Collision Exception
A collision with a particle (e.g. an electron) can still excite the atom if the particle has kinetic energy larger than the required excitation energy (it transfers just enough and keeps the rest).

Example 3: Can a 10 eV photon ionize a hydrogen atom in the first excited state?

Ionization minimum energy from \( n = 2 \): \( E_{\min} = 0 - (-3.40) = 3.40\,\text{eV} \)

Since \( 10\,\text{eV} \ge 3.40\,\text{eV} \), yes the atom can absorb the photon and becomes ionized. Extra energy becomes kinetic energy.

6. Common traps

Warning / Trap
  • Excitation vs ionization: excitation needs an exact energy difference; ionization needs energy ≥ a minimum value.
  • “Photon loses some energy and leaves”: for ionization, the whole photon is absorbed if it causes ionization; extra energy becomes kinetic energy after ionization.
  • Mixing up signs: energy levels are negative; ionization energy from level \( n \) is \( 0 - E_n \) (a positive value).
  • Over-memorising series names (Lyman, Balmer, Paschen): not required in the syllabus.
