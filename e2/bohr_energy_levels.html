Bohr’s model keeps the idea of an electron orbiting the nucleus (a “classical” picture), but adds “quantum” rules so that only certain orbits and energies are allowed. This explains why hydrogen shows discrete spectral lines.

1. What Bohr’s model fixed

Key idea

  • Rutherford model problem: orbiting electrons should radiate continuously and spiral into the nucleus → cannot explain stable atoms or discrete line spectra.
  • Bohr’s solution: electrons can occupy only discrete orbits (labelled by n = 1, 2, 3, …). Each orbit corresponds to a discrete total energy E_n.
(In DSE wording: “energy levels of the hydrogen atom” / “energy levels of the electron” are equivalent.)

2. Bohr postulates: quantum vs classical aspects

(A) Quantization of angular momentum (quantum aspect)

Only orbits satisfying the Bohr quantization condition are allowed: me v r = n h / (2π) (equivalently 2π me v r = n h), where n = 1, 2, 3, … is the quantum number.

Basic knowledge of angular momentum = mvr is expected. (Derivation is not required.)

(B) Allowed orbits (classical picture, but restricted)

  • Electron moves in a circular orbit around the nucleus (classical picture).
  • But only some orbits are allowed because of the quantization condition (quantum rule).

(C) Electron transitions (photon emission / absorption)

  • Emission: electron drops from higher level E_a to lower level E_b, emitting a photon with energy hf = E_a - E_b.
  • Absorption (excitation): electron jumps from lower level to higher level only if it absorbs a photon with energy exactly equal to the level difference.

3. Energy levels of the hydrogen atom

Bohr energy equation (hydrogen)

En = −13.6 eV / n2 (n = 1, 2, 3, …)
  • The negative sign means the electron is bound to the nucleus; energy must be supplied to remove it to infinity.
  • At n = ∞, the electron is free and E∞ = 0.
  • Energy levels get closer together as n increases.

4. Excitation energy vs ionization energy

Excitation (needs an exact amount of energy)

  • Excitation energy from level b to a is ΔE = E_a - E_b.
  • A photon can excite the atom only if its energy equals ΔE exactly.

Ionization (minimum energy, not exact)

  • Ionization means the electron escapes completely (to n = ∞ with E∞ = 0).
  • Minimum energy to ionize an atom in state n: Emin = E∞ - E_n = 0 - E_n.
  • The atom can absorb any photon with energy ≥ Emin. Any extra energy becomes the kinetic energy of the electron and the nucleus (or ion) after ionization.

Quick checkpoints (DSE style)

  • From n=1 to n=2: ΔE = (-3.40) - (-13.6) = 10.2\ \text{eV}.
  • Ionization energy from ground state: Emin = 0 - (-13.6) = 13.6\ \text{eV}.
  • Ionization energy from first excited state (n=2): Emin = 0 - (-3.40) = 3.40\ \text{eV} (smaller than 13.6 eV).

5. Worked examples (concise, marking-scheme style)

Example 1: Find the energy level at n = 3

Formula: En = −13.6 eV / n2

Substitute: E_3 = -13.6 / 3^2 = -13.6 / 9 = -1.51\ \text{eV}

Answer: E_3 = -1.51\ \text{eV}

Example 2: Can a 12.2 eV photon excite a hydrogen atom in the ground state to n = 3?

Excitation requires exact energy: ΔE = E_3 - E_1

Using levels: ΔE = (-1.51) - (-13.6) = 12.09\ \text{eV}

Compare: Photon energy = 12.2 eV ≠ 12.09 eV

Conclusion: No, the photon cannot excite the atom (it will not be absorbed for excitation).

Note: A collision with a particle (e.g. an electron) can still excite the atom if the particle has KE larger than the required excitation energy.

Example 3: Can a 10 eV photon ionize a hydrogen atom in the first excited state?

Ionization minimum energy from n = 2: Emin = 0 - E_2 = 0 - (-3.40) = 3.40\ \text{eV}

Since 10 eV ≥ 3.40 eV, yes the atom can absorb the photon and becomes ionized. Extra energy becomes kinetic energy after ionization.

6. Common traps (high-frequency DSE mistakes)

Trap 1: “Photon loses some energy and leaves”

  • Wrong idea: photon partly transfers energy to the atom and then continues with lower energy.
  • Correct (ionization): if photon energy ≥ ionization energy, the whole photon is absorbed and the extra energy becomes kinetic energy after ionization.

Trap 2: Confusing excitation with ionization

  • Excitation: photon energy must equal the level difference exactly.
  • Ionization: photon energy can be any value ≥ minimum required (not exact).
