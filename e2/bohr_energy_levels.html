3. Bohr’s Model and Line Spectra

Explain the special features of hydrogen line spectra using quantised energy levels. Apply Bohr’s energy-level formula and the wavelength expression to typical HKDSE questions.

Exam focus: line spectra, En, 1/λ formula Skills: reading energy-level diagrams, comparing wavelength / energy

3.1 Line spectra

  • Emission spectrum: bright coloured lines on a dark background (e.g. low-pressure hydrogen gas in a discharge tube).
  • Absorption spectrum: dark lines on a continuous background (e.g. dark lines in the Sun’s spectrum when light from the hot interior passes through cooler gas in the outer layers).
  • In an absorption spectrum, photons of particular wavelengths are absorbed by atoms in a cooler gas, exciting electrons to higher levels; the atoms then re-emit photons of the same energy in random directions, so along the original direction of the beam these wavelengths are missing and appear as dark lines.
  • Each element has its own characteristic pattern of spectral lines, acting like a fingerprint for that element.
  • For hydrogen, lines in a series get closer together toward shorter wavelength (higher energy) and converge to a series limit (corresponding to transitions from higher and higher levels to the same lower level).
  • Line spectra show that atoms emit or absorb photons of discrete energies only → strong evidence that atomic energy levels are quantised.
  • Names of spectral series (Lyman, Balmer, Paschen, etc.) are not required in HKDSE.

3.2 Bohr’s postulates for hydrogen

  • Electrons move in certain allowed circular stationary orbits around the nucleus without radiating energy.
  • Angular momentum of an electron in an allowed orbit is quantised: 2π me vn rn = n h,   n = 1, 2, 3, ...
  • When an electron jumps between energy levels, radiation is emitted or absorbed: h f = Ehigh - Elow
  • Classical aspects: electron moves in a circular orbit under Coulomb attraction, similar to a planet orbiting the Sun.
  • Quantum aspects: only certain orbits (energy levels) are allowed; angular momentum is restricted to discrete values L = n h 2π and radiation is emitted / absorbed only when the electron jumps between levels.
  • The condition 2π me vn rn = n h represents quantisation of angular momentum of the electron.
In HKDSE, you are not required to derive Bohr’s postulates. You should be able to state them and distinguish clearly which parts are classical and which are quantum.

3.3 Energy levels of hydrogen

Total energy of the electron in the n-th Bohr orbit:

En = - 13.6 eV n2 ,   n = 1, 2, 3, ...
  • n = 1: ground state (lowest energy).
  • n > 1: excited states.
  • As n increases, levels become closer together and energy approaches 0 eV from below; E = 0 corresponds to the electron just free from the atom.
  • Ionisation energy: energy required to move an electron from a bound level (often the ground state) to E = 0 (free electron).
  • Excitation energy: energy required to move an electron from a lower bound level to a higher bound level.
  • If a photon has energy exactly equal to the difference between two bound levels, the atom is excited. If the photon energy is greater than the ionisation energy from that level, the atom is ionised and the electron is liberated with kinetic energy equal to the excess.
  • Energy is often expressed in electron-volt: 1 eV = 1.60 × 10−19 J.
In exam questions, the negative sign in En means the electron is bound to the nucleus. We choose the reference level so that a free electron at infinite separation has E = 0. Work must be done to move an electron from a bound level (e.g. n = 1 or 3) to E = 0, so the ionisation energy from that level is E∞ − En = 0 − En = |En|, which is always a positive quantity (e.g. 0.85 eV from n = 3).

3.4 Wavelength of hydrogen spectral lines

For a transition between levels a and b (Bohr energy levels):

Emission (electron falls from a to b, with a > b)

1 λab = 13.6 eV h c × ( 1 b2 − 1 a2 )

Absorption (electron rises from b to a, with a > b)

1 λba = 13.6 eV h c × ( 1 a2 − 1 b2 )

Typical exam skills:

  • Draw simple energy-level diagrams for hydrogen and mark transitions correctly.
  • Compare photon wavelengths, frequencies and energies for different transitions.
  • Use E = h f = h c / λ and the Bohr energy formula to solve numerical problems.
You are expected to be able to obtain the 1/λ expression by combining En = −13.6 eV / n2 with E = h f = h c / λ, but the algebraic steps are usually brief in HKDSE.

3.5 Key terms for HKDSE (Bohr model & spectra)

Line spectrum
A spectrum consisting of discrete bright or dark lines, each corresponding to radiation of a specific wavelength or frequency, produced when atoms emit or absorb photons during transitions between quantised energy levels.

HKDSE common traps & mistakes (Bohr model & spectra)

  • Confusing emission and absorption processes: for emission the electron falls from a higher to a lower level and the atom emits a photon; for absorption the electron goes from a lower to a higher level.
  • Putting the levels in the wrong order in the wavelength expression; for emission with a > b, the factor in brackets must be 1/b2 − 1/a2.
  • Forgetting that bound energy levels are negative and approach 0 eV as n increases; the ground state (n = 1) has the most negative energy.
  • Mixing up excitation and ionisation, or saying that an atom absorbs only part of a photon’s energy. If the photon energy is not equal to an allowed energy difference, it is not absorbed.
  • Drawing energy-level diagrams in a non-standard way (levels not horizontal, wrong ordering of energies), making it difficult to show correct transitions for visible lines.
  • Stating ionisation energy as a negative value (e.g. I.E. = E3 = −0.85 eV). Ionisation energy is the energy required to remove an electron, so it should be a positive quantity (e.g. 0.85 eV from n = 3).
  • Explaining why Bohr energy levels are negative without mentioning the total energy of a bound electron and the convention that a free electron at infinity has E = 0. Simply saying “En < 0” is not enough for full credit.
