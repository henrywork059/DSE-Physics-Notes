1. What Bohr’s model fixed Motivation

  • Rutherford model problem: orbiting electrons should radiate continuously and spiral into the nucleus → cannot explain stable atoms and discrete line spectra.
  • Bohr’s idea: electrons can occupy only discrete orbits (labelled by n = 1, 2, 3, …). Each orbit corresponds to a discrete total energy En.
HKDSE wording: “energy levels of the hydrogen atom” and “energy levels of the electron” are used interchangeably.

2. Bohr postulates Postulates

Classical aspect

  • Electrons are treated as particles moving in circular orbits around the nucleus.
  • The centripetal force is provided by the electric (Coulomb) force of attraction between the electron and the nucleus.

Quantum aspects

  • Only certain discrete orbits are allowed (labelled by n = 1, 2, 3, …). These are called stationary orbits.
  • An electron in a stationary orbit does not radiate electromagnetic energy.
  • Radiation is emitted or absorbed only when the electron jumps between energy levels.
h f = h cλ = Ea − Eb
Meaning: photon energy equals the difference between a higher and a lower energy level. Emission: higher → lower. Absorption (excitation): lower → higher (photon energy must match the level difference exactly).

Quantization condition (Bohr)

me v r = n h2π   (n = 1, 2, 3, …)
2π me v r = n h
HKDSE note: Basic knowledge: angular momentum = m v r. Derivation is not required.

3. Energy levels of the hydrogen atom Energy levels

Bohr energy equation (hydrogen)

En = −13.6 eVn2 (n = 1, 2, 3, …)
  • The negative sign means the electron is bound to the nucleus; energy must be supplied to remove it to infinity.
  • At n = ∞, the electron is free and E∞ = 0.
  • Energy levels get closer together as n increases.

4. Excitation energy vs ionization energy Exam

Excitation (exact energy needed)

  • Excitation from level b to a requires the exact energy difference:
ΔE = Ea − Eb
  • A photon can excite the atom only if its energy equals ΔE exactly.

Ionization (minimum energy, not exact)

  • Ionization means the electron escapes completely to n = ∞ where E∞ = 0.
  • Minimum energy to ionize an atom in state n:
Emin = E∞ − En = 0 − En
  • The atom can absorb any photon with energy ≥ Emin. Any extra energy becomes kinetic energy after ionization.

Quick checkpoints

  • From n = 1 to n = 2: ΔE = (−3.40) − (−13.6) = 10.2 eV.
  • Ionization energy from ground state: Emin = 0 − (−13.6) = 13.6 eV.
  • Ionization energy from n = 2: Emin = 0 − (−3.40) = 3.40 eV.

5. Worked examples Skills

Example 1: Find the energy level at n = 3

Formula: En = −13.6 eVn2

Substitute: E3 = −13.6 / 32 = −13.6 / 9 = −1.51 eV

Answer: E3 = −1.51 eV

Example 2: Can a 12.2 eV photon excite a hydrogen atom in the ground state to n = 3?

Excitation requires exact energy: ΔE = E3 − E1

Using levels: ΔE = (−1.51) − (−13.6) = 12.09 eV

Compare: 12.2 eV ≠ 12.09 eV

Conclusion: No, the photon cannot excite the atom (it will not be absorbed for excitation).

Note: A collision with a particle (e.g. an electron) can still excite the atom if the particle has kinetic energy larger than the required excitation energy.

Example 3: Can a 10 eV photon ionize a hydrogen atom in the first excited state?

Ionization minimum energy from n = 2: Emin = 0 − (−3.40) = 3.40 eV

Since 10 eV ≥ 3.40 eV, yes the atom can absorb the photon and becomes ionized. Extra energy becomes kinetic energy after ionization.

6. Common traps Traps

  • Excitation vs ionization: excitation needs an exact energy difference; ionization needs energy ≥ a minimum value.
  • “Photon loses some energy and leaves”: for ionization, the whole photon is absorbed if it causes ionization; extra energy becomes kinetic energy after ionization.
  • Mixing up signs: energy levels are negative; ionization energy from level n is 0 − En (a positive value).
  • Over-memorising series names (Lyman, Balmer, Paschen): not required in the syllabus.
