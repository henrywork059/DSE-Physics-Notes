1. I–V curve and stopping potential

What is stopping potential?

  • Apply a retarding potential (anode negative) to oppose photoelectrons.
  • The stopping potential Vs is the magnitude of the retarding potential that just stops the most energetic photoelectrons so the photocurrent becomes zero.
How to measure in the lab (DSE wording): adjust the output voltage until the ammeter reading just drops to zero. The voltmeter reading gives the stopping potential Vs.

Link to maximum kinetic energy

Kmax = eVs

Typical photocurrent (I) vs applied p.d. (V)

Reading tip: Vs is the magnitude of the x-value where the current first becomes zero on the retarding side.

2. Einstein’s photoelectric equation

Main equation

Kmax = h f − φ
eVs = h f − φ
  • h f is the energy of one photon.
  • φ (work function) is the minimum energy needed for an electron to escape from the metal surface.
  • Any remaining energy becomes the maximum kinetic energy of the emitted photoelectrons.

Threshold condition

At threshold, photoelectrons just escape with Kmax = 0:

φ = h f0

So f0 depends on the metal only. Larger φ → larger f0.

Using wavelength

Since c = fλ:

Kmax = h f − φ = h cλ − φ
HKDSE note: You are not required to learn the detailed design/history of Millikan’s photoelectric experiment. However, using eVs = h f − φ to find h, f0, or φ from graphs/data is examinable.

3. Straight-line graphs you must recognise

(A) Graph of Vs against f

Vs = he f − φe
Feature on the graph Meaning
Slope h/e (same for all metals)
x-intercept threshold frequency f0
y-intercept −φ/e (negative)

Vs–f line: same slope, different intercepts

Tip: larger φ → larger f0 (shift right).
From the slope: if the slope is measured in V Hz−1, then h = (slope) × e (so slope is a convenient way to estimate h).

(B) Graph of Kmax against 1λ

Useful when the question gives wavelength instead of frequency.

Kmax = h c × 1λ − φ
  • If you plot Kmax (in J) against 1λ, the slope is h c.
  • The x-intercept gives 1λ0. Then f0 = c/λ0.

4. Worked examples (marking-scheme style)

Example 1: Use a Vs–f graph to estimate h and φ

  • Two points on the best-fit line are ( f = 5 × 1014 Hz, Vs = 0 V ) and ( f = 8 × 1014 Hz, Vs = 1.2 V ).
slope = 1.2 − 0(8 − 5) × 1014 = 4.0 × 10−15 V Hz−1
h = (slope) × e = (4.0 × 10−15)(1.60 × 10−19) = 6.4 × 10−34 J s
f0 = 5 × 1014 Hz
φ = h f0 = (6.4 × 10−34)(5 × 1014) = 3.2 × 10−19 J = 2.0 eV
  • Caution: the straight line does not pass through the origin. Do not use (0, 0) to find the slope.
  • Caution: pay attention to the scale factor (e.g. f / 1014 Hz) when reading the graph.

Example 2: Find stopping potential from frequency and work function

Given f and φ, first find Kmax, then use Kmax = eVs.

eVs = h f − φ
  • If h f ≤ φ, then no photoelectrons are emitted, so there is no stopping potential to measure.
  • If h f > φ, then Vs = h f − φe.
Constants often provided: c = 3.00 × 108 m s−1, e = 1.60 × 10−19 C, h = 6.63 × 10−34 J s.

5. Key terms

Einstein’s photoelectric equation: Kmax = h f − φ. Using stopping potential, eVs = h f − φ.

Kmax: maximum kinetic energy among all emitted photoelectrons. It relates to stopping potential by Kmax = eVs.

Stopping potential Vs: the smallest retarding potential magnitude that just stops the most energetic photoelectrons so the photocurrent falls to zero.

Saturation current: when the anode is sufficiently positive, (almost) all emitted photoelectrons are collected, so the photocurrent reaches a maximum (constant) value.

Work function φ: minimum energy needed for an electron to escape from a metal surface. It is a property of the material.

Threshold frequency f0: minimum frequency required to produce photoelectrons. At threshold, φ = h f0.

Planck constant h: relates photon energy and frequency: E = h f. From a Vs–f graph, slope = h/e.

Retarding potential: an applied p.d. that opposes the motion of photoelectrons (anode negative), reducing the photocurrent.

6. Common traps (high-frequency DSE mistakes)

  • Mixing up Kmax, Vs and φ: correct links are Kmax = eVs and Kmax = h f − φ.
  • Sign mistake with voltage: stopping happens for a retarding p.d.; in many questions you may see stopping at V = −Vs.
  • Assuming the Vs–f line passes the origin: it does not. The y-intercept is negative (about −φ/e).
  • Using the wrong slope: for Vs against f, slope = h/e (not h, not φ/e).
  • Unit mistakes: forgetting factors like 1014 on the frequency axis, and mixing eV with J without conversion.
  • Intensity confusion: changing intensity mainly changes the photocurrent (and saturation current), not Vs (at fixed f).
