1. \( I \)–\( V \) curve and stopping potential

What is stopping potential?

  • Apply a retarding potential (anode negative) to oppose photoelectrons.
  • The stopping potential \( V_s \) is the magnitude of the retarding potential that just stops the most energetic photoelectrons so the photocurrent becomes zero.
HKDSE Lab Method
How to measure in the lab: adjust the output voltage until the ammeter reading just drops to zero. The voltmeter reading gives the stopping potential \( V_s \).

Link to maximum kinetic energy

$$ K_{\max} = eV_s $$

Typical photocurrent (\( I \)) vs applied p.d. (\( V \))

Reading tip: \( V_s \) is the magnitude of the x-value where the current first becomes zero on the retarding side.

2. Einstein’s photoelectric equation

Main equation

$$ K_{\max} = hf - \phi $$ $$ eV_s = hf - \phi $$
  • \( hf \) is the energy of one photon.
  • \( \phi \) (work function) is the minimum energy needed for an electron to escape from the metal surface.
  • Any remaining energy becomes the maximum kinetic energy of the emitted photoelectrons.

Threshold condition

At threshold, photoelectrons just escape with \( K_{\max} = 0 \):

$$ \phi = hf_0 $$

So \( f_0 \) depends on the metal only. Larger \( \phi \) → larger \( f_0 \).

Using wavelength

Since \( c = f\lambda \):

$$ K_{\max} = hf - \phi = \frac{hc}{\lambda} - \phi $$
HKDSE Note
You are not required to learn the detailed design/history of Millikan’s photoelectric experiment. However, using \( eV_s = hf - \phi \) to find \( h \), \( f_0 \), or \( \phi \) from graphs/data is examinable.

3. Straight-line graphs you must recognise

(A) Graph of \( V_s \) against \( f \)

$$ V_s = \left(\frac{h}{e}\right)f - \frac{\phi}{e} $$
Feature Meaning
Slope \( h/e \) (constant, same for all metals)
x-intercept Threshold frequency \( f_0 \)
y-intercept \( -\phi/e \) (negative value)

\( V_s \)–\( f \) line: same slope, different intercepts

Tip: larger \( \phi \) → larger \( f_0 \) (shifts right). Slope is always \( h/e \).

Slope Calculation
From the slope: if the slope is measured in \( \text{V Hz}^{-1} \), then $$ h = \text{slope} \times e $$ This is a convenient way to estimate the Planck constant \( h \).

(B) Graph of \( K_{\max} \) against \( 1/\lambda \)

Useful when the question gives wavelength instead of frequency.

$$ K_{\max} = hc \left(\frac{1}{\lambda}\right) - \phi $$
  • If you plot \( K_{\max} \) (in J) against \( 1/\lambda \), the slope is \( hc \).
  • The x-intercept gives \( 1/\lambda_0 \). Then \( f_0 = c/\lambda_0 \).

4. Worked examples (marking-scheme style)

Example 1: Use a \( V_s \)–\( f \) graph to estimate \( h \) and \( \phi \)

  • Two points on the best-fit line: \( (f = 5 \times 10^{14}\,\text{Hz}, V_s = 0\,\text{V}) \) and \( (f = 8 \times 10^{14}\,\text{Hz}, V_s = 1.2\,\text{V}) \).
$$ \text{Slope} = \frac{1.2 - 0}{(8 - 5) \times 10^{14}} = 4.0 \times 10^{-15}\,\text{V Hz}^{-1} $$ $$ h = \text{Slope} \times e = (4.0 \times 10^{-15})(1.60 \times 10^{-19}) = 6.4 \times 10^{-34}\,\text{J s} $$ $$ f_0 = 5 \times 10^{14}\,\text{Hz} $$ $$ \phi = hf_0 = (6.4 \times 10^{-34})(5 \times 10^{14}) = 3.2 \times 10^{-19}\,\text{J} = 2.0\,\text{eV} $$
Caution
  • The straight line does not pass through the origin. Do not use (0, 0) to find the slope.
  • Pay attention to the scale factor (e.g. \( f / 10^{14}\,\text{Hz} \)) when reading the graph.

Example 2: Find stopping potential from frequency and work function

Given \( f \) and \( \phi \), first find \( K_{\max} \), then use \( K_{\max} = eV_s \).

$$ eV_s = hf - \phi $$
  • If \( hf \le \phi \), then no photoelectrons are emitted → no stopping potential.
  • If \( hf > \phi \), then \( V_s = \frac{hf - \phi}{e} \).
Constants
Usually provided: \( c = 3.00 \times 10^8\,\text{m s}^{-1} \), \( e = 1.60 \times 10^{-19}\,\text{C} \), \( h = 6.63 \times 10^{-34}\,\text{J s} \).

5. Key terms

Einstein’s photoelectric equation: \( K_{\max} = hf - \phi \). Using stopping potential, \( eV_s = hf - \phi \).

\( K_{\max} \): maximum kinetic energy among all emitted photoelectrons. It relates to stopping potential by \( K_{\max} = eV_s \).

Stopping potential \( V_s \): the smallest retarding potential magnitude that just stops the most energetic photoelectrons so the photocurrent falls to zero.

Saturation current: when the anode is sufficiently positive, (almost) all emitted photoelectrons are collected, so the photocurrent reaches a maximum (constant) value.

Work function \( \phi \): minimum energy needed for an electron to escape from a metal surface. It is a property of the material.

Threshold frequency \( f_0 \): minimum frequency required to produce photoelectrons. At threshold, \( \phi = hf_0 \).

Planck constant \( h \): relates photon energy and frequency: \( E = hf \). From a \( V_s \)–\( f \) graph, slope = \( h/e \).

Retarding potential: an applied p.d. that opposes the motion of photoelectrons (anode negative), reducing the photocurrent.

6. Common traps (high-frequency DSE mistakes)

Warning / Trap
  • Mixing up \( K_{\max} \), \( V_s \) and \( \phi \): correct links are \( K_{\max} = eV_s \) and \( K_{\max} = hf - \phi \).
  • Sign mistake with voltage: stopping happens for a retarding p.d.; in many questions you may see stopping at \( V = -V_s \).
  • Assuming the \( V_s \)–\( f \) line passes the origin: it does not. The y-intercept is negative (about \( -\phi/e \)).
  • Using the wrong slope: for \( V_s \) against \( f \), slope = \( h/e \) (not \( h \), not \( \phi/e \)).
  • Unit mistakes: forgetting factors like \( 10^{14} \) on the frequency axis, and mixing eV with J without conversion.
  • Intensity confusion: changing intensity mainly changes the photocurrent (and saturation current), not \( V_s \) (at fixed \( f \)).
